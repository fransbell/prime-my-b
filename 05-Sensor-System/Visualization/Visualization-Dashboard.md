# Visualization & Dashboard Design

## 1. Overview

Visualization is the critical bridge between raw sensor data and actionable farming decisions. A network of soil moisture sensors, weather stations, and leaf wetness detectors produces hundreds of readings per hour, but these numbers are meaningless to a coffee farmer in Mae Hong Son unless they are transformed into clear, timely, and context-rich visual representations. The visualization layer is where the entire sensor system delivers its return on investment: a farmer glances at a phone screen and immediately knows whether to irrigate, whether Coffee Leaf Rust risk is elevated, or whether conditions are optimal for harvest.

The dashboard must be **mobile-first** because over 80% of Thai farmers access the internet primarily through smartphones. Desktop-only dashboards—common in research and corporate settings—are effectively invisible to the target audience. Every design decision, from font sizes to chart dimensions, must be optimized for a 6-inch touchscreen held in one hand under bright tropical sunlight. The interface must also be **multilingual**, presenting all labels, alerts, and recommendations in Thai as the primary language with English as a secondary option for researchers and international buyers. Most critically, the dashboard must be designed for **quick decision-making, not data exploration**. Farmers do not need to pore over hundreds of data points; they need to know "What is happening right now?" and "What should I do about it?" Every chart, every color, every notification must serve those two questions. The design philosophy is inspired by aviation cockpit displays: critical information is always visible, warnings demand attention, and detail is available on demand but never cluttering the primary view. See [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]] for the threshold values that drive all visual status indicators, and [[Decision-Logic]] for the rules that translate sensor readings into recommended actions.

## 2. Design Principles

### Mobile-First
Over 80% of Thai farmers access the internet via smartphone, and many do not own a laptop or desktop computer. The dashboard must be designed for 6-inch screens as the primary viewport. Touch targets must be at least 44×44 pixels (per Apple Human Interface Guidelines), text must be legible in bright outdoor sunlight (minimum 16sp for body text, 24sp for key values), and horizontal scrolling must be eliminated entirely. Every screen must be fully functional in portrait orientation, which is how 95% of users hold their phones.

### Color-Coded Status
A four-tier color system provides instant status recognition across every screen and component:

| Level | Color | Hex Code | Meaning |
|-------|-------|----------|---------|
| INFO | 🟢 Green | `#2E7D32` | All readings within optimal range |
| WARNING | 🟡 Yellow | `#F9A825` | One or more readings approaching threshold |
| CRITICAL | 🟠 Orange | `#EF6C00` | Action required; reading at or beyond threshold |
| EMERGENCY | 🔴 Red | `#C62828` | Immediate action required; crop at risk |

This color scheme must be applied consistently across every chart background, alert banner, sensor readout, and map marker. The human brain processes color faster than text, and a farmer should be able to assess overall farm health at a single glance. The color palette is also chosen to be distinguishable by users with the most common forms of color vision deficiency (deuteranopia and protanopia), with shape and icon differentiation as a secondary cue.

### Progressive Disclosure
Information is presented in three layers: **Summary → Detail → Raw Data**. The home screen shows only the current status and next recommended action. Tapping a sensor card reveals a detail view with time-series charts and trend information. Tapping again exposes the raw numerical data and sensor metadata. This prevents information overload while ensuring no data is ever more than two taps away. The progressive disclosure model maps directly to the decision hierarchy in [[Decision-Logic]]: the summary view tells you what to do, the detail view tells you why, and the raw data lets you verify.

### Action-Oriented
Every chart and data display must answer the question "What should I do?" A soil moisture chart that merely shows 28% VWC is incomplete; it must also show the optimal range (30–45%), indicate that current moisture is below optimal, and recommend "Consider irrigating within 24 hours." Actionable recommendations are generated by the [[Decision-Logic]] engine and displayed alongside every sensor reading. If no action is needed, the display should explicitly state "No action needed — readings are optimal" to provide reassurance.

### Offline-Capable
Rural Northern Thailand has inconsistent cellular and internet connectivity. Many farms in Chiang Mai, Mae Hong Son, and Chiang Rai provinces have 4G signal only at certain spots on the property, and connectivity can drop for hours during monsoon storms. The app must cache the last 7 days of sensor data in a local SQLite database and render all charts from cached data when offline. A subtle banner ("Offline — showing cached data from 2h ago") must inform the user when the display is not live. When connectivity is restored, the app must automatically sync accumulated data in the background using delta compression to minimize data transfer.

### Thai Language First
All labels, alerts, recommendations, and UI elements must be presented in Thai (ภาษาไทย) as the default language. English is available as a secondary option for researchers, agronomists, and international stakeholders. Number formatting must use the Thai locale (e.g., comma as thousands separator). Date formatting should follow the Buddhist calendar (พ.ศ.) as an option, with Gregorian (ค.ศ.) as default. Alert messages must be written in plain, conversational Thai that a farmer with basic education can understand—avoiding technical jargon like "volumetric water content" in favor of "ความชื้นในดิน" (soil moisture).

### Low Bandwidth
Data transfer must be minimized for users on limited mobile data plans. Sensor readings should be delivered as compressed JSON (gzip), chart rendering must happen entirely on-device (no server-side image generation), and API responses should use field filtering so the client only receives the data it needs. A typical 5-minute sensor update payload should not exceed 2 KB compressed. Image assets must be vector (SVG) or compressed WebP. The app should also support a "low data mode" that reduces refresh frequency from 5 minutes to 15 minutes and disables heat map overlays.

## 3. Dashboard Layout Design

### A. Home Screen (Farmer's Overview)

The home screen is the most important screen in the entire application. It must communicate the farm's current status and the most urgent action in under 3 seconds of visual scanning.

**Top Banner**: A full-width status bar displaying the current highest alert level with a corresponding color background and icon (🟢🟡🟠🔴). Below the icon, a single line of Thai text summarizes the most important action, for example: "⚠️ ควรรดน้ำภายใน 24 ชั่วโมง — ความชื้นในดินต่ำ" (Should irrigate within 24 hours — soil moisture low). If no alerts are active, the banner shows green with: "✅ สภาพฟาร์มปกติ — ไม่ต้องทำอะไรเพิ่มเติม" (Farm conditions normal — no action needed). Tapping the banner navigates to the Alerts Screen.

**Weather NOW Card**: Displays current temperature (°C), relative humidity (%), rainfall today (mm), and wind speed/direction. Each value is color-coded against its threshold (see [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]]). A small sparkline shows the 24-hour trend for each metric. The card uses a subtle gradient background that shifts from blue (cool/wet) to orange (hot/dry) based on current conditions.

**Soil NOW Card**: Displays current volumetric water content (%), soil temperature (°C), and soil pH. Each zone's moisture is shown as a horizontal bar with the optimal range highlighted. If multiple zones exist, a compact zone-selector tab allows switching between zones without leaving the home screen.

**Disease Risk Card**: Prominently displays the current Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR) risk level on a four-tier scale: LOW / MODERATE / HIGH / EXTREME. The risk is computed from the combined readings of leaf wetness duration, humidity, and temperature per the [[Decision-Logic]] rules. A small badge shows the dominant risk factor (e.g., "LWD: 8h" or "RH > 90%"). Tapping the card opens the Disease Risk detail view with factor breakdown.

**Quick Actions**: A row of four tappable buttons providing one-tap access to the most common farmer tasks: "รดน้ำ" (Irrigate Now), "ตรวจโรค" (Check Disease), "เก็บเกี่ยว" (Harvest Conditions), "แจ้งเตือน" (View Alerts). These buttons are always visible at the bottom of the home screen and do not scroll away.

**7-Day Forecast Mini-Chart**: A compact combined area chart at the bottom of the home screen showing predicted temperature range (shaded band) and daily rainfall (bar overlay) for the next 7 days. This is based on sensor trend extrapolation and, when available, government weather API data. Threshold lines for heat stress and frost risk are overlaid.

### B. Sensor Detail Screen

When a farmer taps any sensor card or metric on the home screen, they enter the Sensor Detail Screen for that specific measurement. This screen provides historical context and deeper analysis.

**Time Series Chart**: The primary visualization is a full-width time series chart showing the selected metric over time. Four time-range tabs are available: 24h / 7d / 30d / 1y. The chart background is divided into horizontal threshold bands: a green band spanning the optimal range, yellow bands for warning zones, and red bands for critical zones (specific values from [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]]). The current reading is marked with a pulsing dot. Pinch-to-zoom and swipe-to-pan gestures allow exploring specific time periods. Long-pressing on the chart reveals a tooltip with the exact timestamp and value at that point.

**Current Value Display**: Above the chart, a large digital readout (48sp font) shows the current value with units. The background color matches the current status level. A trend arrow (↑ rising, → stable, ↓ falling) and the rate of change (e.g., "+2.3%/h") appear beside the value.

**Min/Max/Average Panel**: Below the chart, a compact panel shows the minimum, maximum, and average values for the selected time period, each with a timestamp for when the min or max occurred.

**Related Recommendations**: A card at the bottom displays context-specific action recommendations generated by the [[Decision-Logic]] engine. For example, if soil moisture is below threshold: "💡 รดน้ำ 20 ลิตรต่อต้น — ความชื้นต่ำกว่าค่าที่เหมาะสม 12%" (Irrigate 20 L/tree — moisture 12% below optimal). Tapping a recommendation can trigger an action (e.g., logging an irrigation event).

**Event Annotations**: The time series chart supports event annotations—vertical markers showing when irrigation, fertilization, spraying, or harvest events occurred. These help farmers correlate their actions with sensor responses, building intuition about cause and effect. Events are logged manually by the farmer or automatically when smart irrigation valves are activated.

### C. Alerts Screen

The Alerts Screen is the command center for all active and recent notifications, designed to ensure no critical issue goes unaddressed.

**Active Alerts List**: Alerts are sorted by severity (EMERGENCY first, then CRITICAL, WARNING, INFO) and within each severity level by time (newest first). Each alert displays: a color-coded severity icon, a concise Thai-language title (e.g., "🔥 อุณหภูมิสูงเกินไป" — Temperature too high), the trigger condition that caused the alert, and the recommended action from [[Alerts-Remediation]]. An "Acknowledge" button marks the alert as seen without dismissing it—useful when multiple farm workers share a device.

**Alert History**: A tab or filter shows resolved alerts from the past 30 days. Each historical alert shows its resolution timestamp, the action taken (if logged), and the sensor values at the time of resolution. This history helps farmers learn which alerts are recurrent and which conditions improve with intervention.

**Alert Detail**: Tapping an alert expands it to show the full trigger logic, all contributing sensor readings, a mini time-series chart showing the metric leading up to the alert, and step-by-step remediation instructions. The detail view also provides a "Mark as Resolved" button and an optional field to log what action was taken.

### D. Farm Map View

For farms with multiple sensor nodes spread across different zones or plots, the Farm Map View provides spatial awareness that list-based views cannot.

**Sensor Locations**: Sensors are displayed as colored circular markers on a map or satellite image of the farm. Each marker's color reflects the current status of that sensor (green/yellow/orange/red). Tapping a marker shows a popup with the sensor name, current reading, and last update time. Long-pressing navigates to the Sensor Detail Screen for that sensor.

**Zone Comparison**: Tapping a named zone (e.g., "แปลงอรุณ" — Plot A) filters the map to show only sensors in that zone and displays a zone-level summary card with average readings and zone-level recommendations.

**Heat Map Overlay**: An optional toggle replaces point markers with a continuous interpolated heat map showing soil moisture or temperature distribution across the farm. The interpolation uses inverse distance weighting (IDW) from sensor node positions. The heat map uses a green-to-red gradient aligned with the status color system. This feature is particularly valuable for identifying dry spots or microclimate zones that a single sensor might miss. Note: the heat map requires at least 4 sensor nodes to produce meaningful interpolation and is disabled on low-bandwidth mode.

### E. Yield & Quality Screen

This screen connects sensor data to the economic outcomes that matter most to farmers, leveraging predictions from [[Yield-Quality-Prediction]].

**Quality Prediction Gauge**: A semi-circular gauge showing the current probability distribution of the coffee quality grade: Specialty (80+ SCA score), Premium (70–79), or Commodity (<70). The needle position indicates the most likely grade, and the colored arc sections show the probability range. The prediction model incorporates current and recent sensor readings, particularly temperature stability, rainfall patterns, and stress events.

**Yield Estimate**: A bullet chart comparing the current projected yield (kg/rai) against the farmer's target yield and the historical average for the farm. The projection is updated daily as the season progresses and new sensor data accumulates. A confidence interval band shows the uncertainty range, which narrows as harvest approaches.

**Key Factors**: A ranked list of sensor-derived factors currently influencing the quality and yield predictions. Positive factors are shown in green (e.g., "✅ Stable DTR — good for cherry development"), negative factors in red (e.g., "❌ Water stress detected — may reduce cherry size"). Each factor links to the corresponding sensor detail screen for deeper investigation.

**Economic Projection**: A summary card showing estimated revenue based on predicted yield × quality grade price, using current market prices for Northern Thai Arabica. This translates abstract sensor readings into the most concrete metric: "คาดว่าจะได้ ฿45,000/ไร่" (Estimated ฿45,000/rai). The economic projection is updated weekly and uses price data from local cooperative and market sources.

## 4. Chart Type Specifications

Each sensor data type demands a specific visualization that communicates its meaning most effectively. The following specifications ensure consistency and clarity across the dashboard.

| Data Type | Chart Type | Key Features | Threshold Display |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Soil Moisture** | Line chart with shaded threshold bands | Green optimal zone (30–45% VWC), yellow warning bands, red critical zones. Fill area under the line for visual weight. | Horizontal colored bands behind the line |
| **Temperature** | Dual-axis line chart (min/max) with DTR fill | Min and max temperature as two lines; the area between them filled to show diurnal temperature range (DTR). DTR > 15°C highlighted as stress zone. | Horizontal threshold lines for frost (< 10°C), heat stress (> 30°C), optimal (18–25°C) |
| **Rainfall** | Bar chart (daily totals) with cumulative line overlay | Blue bars for daily rainfall; orange cumulative line. 7-day rolling average shown as dashed line. | Horizontal line at 50 mm/week (excess) and 10 mm/week (drought) |
| **Humidity** | Line chart with disease risk zone shading | Humidity trace with background zones: green (< 70%), yellow (70–85%), orange (85–95%), red (> 95%). CLR risk increases sharply above 85%. | Background color bands |
| **Leaf Wetness Duration** | Stacked bar (wet hours per day) | Each bar represents one day; the bar is segmented into "dry hours" (light) and "wet hours" (dark blue). CLR risk color coding on the wet portion: < 4h green, 4–8h yellow, > 8h red. | Horizontal line at 4h and 8h LWD thresholds |
| **Shade/Light** | Gauge chart (0–100%) | Semi-circular gauge with needle showing current light level. Optimal shade zone (40–60% shade for Arabica) marked in green. | Colored arc segments: < 30% shade (red — too exposed), 30–70% (green — optimal), > 70% (yellow — too shaded) |
| **Wind** | Compass rose (direction) + speed line chart | Compass rose showing wind direction distribution over 24h. Separate line chart for wind speed with gust peaks marked. | Horizontal line at 5 m/s (spray drift risk) and 15 m/s (tree damage risk) |
| **Disease Risk** | Radar/spider chart | Four axes: Humidity, Temperature (suitability for CLR), LWD, Rainfall frequency. The filled area shows current risk profile; larger area = higher risk. Optimal (low risk) reference polygon overlaid. | Reference polygon at boundary between LOW and MODERATE risk |
| **Yield Prediction** | Bullet chart | Vertical bullet: predicted yield (marker), target yield (line), last year (secondary marker), with colored performance zones (red < 60% target, yellow 60–80%, green > 80%). | Performance zone boundaries at 60% and 80% of target |
| **Quality Prediction** | Horizontal stacked bar | Three segments showing probability: Specialty (green), Premium (yellow), Commodity (red). The dominant segment's label is displayed prominently. | Boundary lines at 50% probability |
| **Soil pH** | Gauge chart | Semi-circular gauge from pH 3.0 to 8.0. Optimal range for Arabica (5.5–6.5) marked in green. | Colored arc: < 5.0 (red — too acidic), 5.0–5.5 (yellow), 5.5–6.5 (green), 6.5–7.0 (yellow), > 7.0 (red) |
| **Alert History** | Stacked bar by severity per week | Each bar represents one week; segments show count of EMERGENCY (red), CRITICAL (orange), WARNING (yellow), and INFO (green) alerts. | None (descriptive chart) |

All charts must support tap-to-inspect (mobile) or hover-tooltip (desktop) for exact values. Charts must be responsive: on phone screens, chart height is fixed at 200px; on tablets, 300px; on desktop, 400px. Chart animations should be subtle (300ms ease) and never interfere with data reading. All chart axes must be labeled in Thai with metric units, and the time axis must respect the Thai locale for date formatting.

## 5. KPI Panel Design

The Key Performance Indicator (KPI) panel is a persistent, always-visible strip on the home screen and every major navigation level. It provides the six most critical summary metrics at a glance, ensuring that even a momentary screen glance communicates farm status.

**Farm Health Score (0–100)**: A composite score computed as a weighted average of all sensor readings against their optimal ranges. The weighting prioritizes factors with the greatest impact on coffee quality and yield: soil moisture (25%), temperature stability/DTR (20%), disease risk (20%), rainfall adequacy (15%), shade level (10%), and soil pH (10%). A score of 90–100 displays green (excellent), 70–89 yellow-green (good), 50–69 yellow (attention needed), 30–49 orange (problems present), 0–29 red (critical). The score is computed by the [[Decision-Logic]] engine and updated every 5 minutes.

**Active Alert Count**: Displayed as a badge showing the number of active alerts broken down by severity: 🔴 0 🟠 1 🟡 3 🟢 2. Tapping the badge navigates to the Alerts Screen. A red dot on the app icon (badge count) shows the total unresolved EMERGENCY + CRITICAL alerts.

**Days Since Last Irrigation (per zone)**: For each irrigated zone, a counter showing how many days have passed since the last irrigation event. The counter turns yellow after 3 days and red after 5 days (adjustable based on crop stage and soil type). This KPI directly supports the irrigation decision loop described in [[Decision-Logic]].

**CLR Risk Level**: The current Coffee Leaf Rust risk status displayed as a colored label: LOW (green), MODERATE (yellow), HIGH (orange), EXTREME (red). This is always visible because CLR is the single most economically damaging disease for Northern Thai Arabica coffee. Tapping navigates to the Disease Risk detail view.

**Harvest Readiness**: A countdown showing the estimated number of days until the next optimal harvest window, based on cherry maturity models that incorporate growing degree days, rainfall patterns, and current sensor readings. When the harvest window is open, this KPI displays "พร้อมเก็บเกี่ยว" (Ready to harvest) in green. This metric is derived from the [[Yield-Quality-Prediction]] model.

**Water Balance**: A single number showing the net water balance (mm) computed as: cumulative rainfall + irrigation − evapotranspiration (ET₀ × crop coefficient). Positive values (surplus) are shown in blue; negative values (deficit) in orange-red. A 7-day trend sparkline accompanies the number. This KPI is essential for irrigation scheduling and is updated hourly.

## 6. Mobile App Technical Specifications

The mobile application is the primary interface for farmers and must be built on a robust, maintainable, and performant technology stack.

**Platform**: React Native or Flutter, targeting iOS 14+ and Android 8.0+ (API level 26). Flutter is preferred for its superior chart rendering performance and consistent UI across platforms, but React Native is acceptable if the development team has stronger JavaScript/TypeScript expertise. Both frameworks support hot reloading for rapid iteration.

**Chart Library**: Apache ECharts (via `echarts` + `react-native-svg` bridge or `flutter_echarts`) is the recommended charting library. ECharts provides rich interactive charts, threshold band rendering, smooth animations, and excellent performance with large datasets (10,000+ data points). As a lightweight alternative for simpler charts, D3.js (via `d3-array` and custom SVG rendering) can be used, but it requires more development effort for the same visual output. ECharts is preferred for its built-in support for gauge charts, radar charts, and bullet charts—all of which are required by the chart specifications in Section 4.

**Data API**: REST API (JSON over HTTPS) for initial data loads and historical queries, combined with WebSocket connections for real-time sensor updates and alert push. The REST API supports field filtering (`?fields=moisture,temperature`), time-range queries (`?from=2026-05-05&to=2026-05-12`), and gzip compression. WebSocket connections use a publish-subscribe model where the client subscribes to specific sensor node IDs and receives delta updates only (not full payloads), minimizing bandwidth.

**Offline Storage**: SQLite database (via `react-native-sqlite-storage` or `sqflite` for Flutter) stores the last 7 days of sensor readings, the last 30 days of alert history, farm configuration, and user preferences. When the app detects network unavailability, it reads from the local cache and renders charts from cached data. A background sync service attempts to reconnect every 60 seconds and, on success, performs a delta sync using the last-known timestamp. Conflict resolution uses "server wins" for sensor data (sensors are the source of truth) and "client wins" for user actions (irrigation logs, alert acknowledgments).

**Push Notifications**: Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) for Android and Apple Push Notification service (APNs) for iOS. Alert notifications are routed through the backend [[Alerts-Remediation]] engine and delivered as high-priority push messages. EMERGENCY alerts bypass the device's "Do Not Disturb" mode (using FCM's `priority: high` and APNs' `interruption-level: critical`). CRITICAL alerts use standard high-priority delivery. WARNING and INFO alerts are batched and delivered in a daily summary at 7:00 AM local time.

**LINE Integration**: LINE Messaging API is a critical channel for Thailand, where LINE has 50+ million users. The LINE bot (described in detail in Section 7) serves as both a notification channel and a lightweight dashboard alternative. The mobile app includes a "Connect LINE" flow that links the farmer's LINE account to their farm profile. Once connected, the backend can push alerts to LINE in addition to (or instead of) native push notifications, based on user preference.

**Data Refresh Strategy**: Real-time (WebSocket) for alerts and EMERGENCY status changes. Every 5 minutes (polling) for sensor readings on the home screen and detail views. Every 15 minutes for KPI scores and predictions. Daily at 6:00 AM for yield/quality predictions and weekly economic projections. The refresh strategy is configurable per-user and automatically adapts based on network conditions (3G/4G/WiFi).

**Screen Size Support**: Primary target is 6-inch smartphones (360–414dp width). Secondary target is 10-inch tablets (768–1024dp width), which use a two-pane layout with the map or chart list on the left and detail view on the right. Desktop browsers (1280dp+) receive the full web dashboard described in Section 8. All layouts are responsive and built with a mobile-first CSS approach.

## 7. Alternative: LINE Bot Dashboard

LINE is the dominant messaging platform in Thailand with over 50 million active users—virtually every Thai farmer who owns a smartphone has LINE installed. Building a LINE bot as a companion to (or lightweight replacement for) the full mobile app dramatically lowers the adoption barrier. Farmers do not need to install a new app, learn a new interface, or grant additional permissions; they simply add the bot as a LINE friend and start interacting.

**Command-Based Interaction**: The bot supports a set of text commands and Quick Reply buttons. Key commands include:

- `/status` or `/สถานะ` → Bot replies with a summary card showing Farm Health Score, current alert level, key sensor readings, and top recommended action. The reply uses a rich Flex Message with color-coded status indicators.
- `/alerts` or `/แจ้งเตือน` → Bot replies with a list of active alerts, sorted by severity. Each alert includes a recommended action.
- `/disease` or `/โรค` → Bot replies with the current CLR risk level and contributing factors.
- `/weather` or `/อากาศ` → Bot replies with current weather conditions and 3-day forecast.
- `/irrigate` or `/รดน้ำ` → Bot replies with irrigation recommendation including water balance and days since last irrigation.

**Proactive Alert Push**: The bot automatically sends push messages for CRITICAL and EMERGENCY alerts. Each push message includes the alert title, trigger condition, and recommended action with a Quick Reply button for acknowledging the alert. INFO and WARNING alerts are not pushed individually; instead, a daily morning summary is sent at 7:00 AM covering all lower-severity events from the past 24 hours. This balance prevents notification fatigue while ensuring urgent issues reach the farmer immediately.

**Quick Reply Menu**: Every bot message includes a Quick Reply footer with the most relevant follow-up actions. For example, a high-temperature alert message includes Quick Reply buttons: "ตรวจสอบความชื้นดิน" (Check soil moisture), "ดูระดับความเสี่ยงโรค" (Check disease risk), "รดน้ำเลย" (Irrigate now). This reduces typing and makes the bot usable even for farmers uncomfortable with text commands.

**Cost**: The LINE Messaging API free tier allows 1,000 push messages per month, which is sufficient for a single farm receiving daily summaries and occasional CRITICAL alerts. For farms or cooperatives needing more messages, the LINE Official Account paid plan starts at approximately ฿700/month for 15,000 messages. The development cost of the LINE bot is minimal (estimated 2–3 developer-weeks) since it reuses the same backend API as the mobile app and web dashboard. Hosting costs are negligible—a serverless function (AWS Lambda or Cloudflare Workers) processing incoming LINE webhooks costs under ฿100/month at typical usage levels.

## 8. Web Dashboard (for Farm Managers/Researchers)

While the mobile app and LINE bot serve the farmer's daily operational needs, a full-featured web dashboard serves farm managers, agricultural researchers, cooperative administrators, and government extension officers who require deeper analytical capabilities and multi-farm oversight.

**Full Desktop Experience**: The web dashboard is a responsive single-page application (SPA) built with React or Vue.js, optimized for desktop screens (1280px+ width). It uses the same ECharts library as the mobile app for consistent chart rendering but takes advantage of the larger screen real estate to display multi-chart dashboards, side-by-side comparisons, and data tables. The left sidebar provides navigation: Dashboard, Sensors, Alerts, Farm Map, Yield & Quality, Reports, Settings.

**Export to CSV/Excel**: All chart data and tabular views include an export button that downloads the current view's data as CSV or Excel (.xlsx). This is essential for researchers who need to perform statistical analysis in R, Python, or Excel, and for cooperatives that must generate reports for buyers or certification bodies. Export respects the user's time-range filter and includes all metadata (sensor IDs, units, timestamps).

**Historical Trend Analysis**: Year-over-year comparison views allow researchers to compare the current season against previous seasons, identifying long-term trends in soil health, temperature patterns, and disease pressure. Anomaly detection highlights periods where current readings deviate significantly from historical norms (±2 standard deviations). Seasonal decomposition charts separate trend, seasonal, and residual components of key metrics.

**Multi-Farm Aggregate View**: For cooperatives managing multiple farms, the dashboard provides an aggregate view showing key metrics across all member farms. A summary table lists each farm's Health Score, CLR risk, and active alert count. Drill-down links navigate to individual farm dashboards. Aggregate statistics (mean, median, range) help identify farms that are outliers—either struggling or excelling—for targeted intervention or knowledge sharing.

**Admin Tools**: The web dashboard includes administrative tools for sensor management (provisioning new nodes, decommissioning failed nodes, updating firmware OTA), user management (adding/removing farmers, assigning roles and permissions), alert rule configuration (adjusting thresholds per-farm or per-zone, setting notification preferences), and data retention policies (archiving old data, setting backup schedules). These tools are access-controlled: farm managers can manage their own farms, while cooperative admins can manage all member farms.

## 9. Data Visualization Best Practices

Effective visualization for agricultural sensor data requires discipline and empathy for the end user. The following best practices are mandatory for all dashboard components.

**Never Show Raw ADC Values**: Sensors often report raw analog-to-digital converter (ADC) readings that must be calibrated and converted before display. A soil moisture sensor might output a 12-bit ADC value of 2048, but the farmer must see "34% VWC"—the calibrated volumetric water content percentage. All conversion formulas and calibration offsets are maintained in the backend [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]] configuration and applied before data reaches the visualization layer. The raw ADC values should be accessible only in the developer/debug view, never in the farmer-facing interface.

**Use Consistent Color Coding**: The Green/Yellow/Orange/Red status system must be applied without exception across every screen, chart, alert, and notification. A farmer who learns that red means "act now" on the home screen should never encounter a red element that means something different on another screen. Color consistency builds trust and reduces cognitive load. Where additional color is needed for data differentiation (e.g., multiple lines on a chart), use blue/purple/teal shades that do not conflict with the status colors.

**Show Thresholds as Visual Bands, Not Just Numbers**: A threshold value displayed as a single horizontal line is easy to miss. Instead, thresholds should be rendered as shaded bands spanning the full width of the chart area, with the optimal zone filled in light green, warning zones in light yellow, and critical zones in light red. This makes the status of any reading immediately apparent from its position within the bands, without requiring the viewer to compare a number against a remembered threshold value. All threshold values are defined in [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]].

**Annotate Charts with Events**: Raw sensor data is most meaningful when correlated with the farmer's actions. Every chart should support event annotations—vertical dashed lines with labels marking irrigation events, fertilizer applications, pesticide spraying, harvest dates, and significant weather events (storms, frost). These annotations help farmers build mental models of cause and effect: "After I irrigated on Tuesday, soil moisture rose by 8% and stayed in the green zone for 4 days." Event annotations also help agronomists diagnose problems by correlating interventions with outcomes.

**Provide Tooltips and Hover Information**: On desktop, hovering over a data point should display a tooltip with the exact timestamp, value, status level, and any event annotation at that point. On mobile, long-pressing a data point should trigger the same tooltip. Tooltips are the primary mechanism for accessing precise values without cluttering the chart with numbers. They should appear within 200ms of the hover/press gesture and disappear when the pointer/finger is removed.

**Keep Charts Simple**: The dashboard is designed for decision-making, not data exploration. Each chart should convey a single primary message. Avoid combining more than three data series on a single chart. Avoid 3D effects, gratuitous animations, or decorative elements that do not convey information. The visual hierarchy should guide the eye to the most important element first: the current value and its status. If a researcher needs complex multi-variable analysis, they should export the data to a statistical tool rather than attempting to read it from a dashboard chart.

## 10. Practical Recommendations

1. **Start with the LINE bot, then build the mobile app.** The LINE bot requires no app installation and reaches farmers through a platform they already use daily. It validates the value proposition and builds user trust before asking farmers to install a dedicated app. Use LINE bot engagement metrics to prioritize which mobile app features to build first.

2. **Field-test all chart designs with real farmers before committing to implementation.** Show paper prototypes or Figma mockups to at least 10 farmers in Chiang Mai and Mae Hong Son. Ask them to interpret the charts and state what action they would take. Revise designs based on their feedback—researchers and farmers interpret visualizations very differently. What seems intuitive to a data scientist may be confusing to a farmer who has never used a line chart.

3. **Implement offline mode from day one, not as an afterthought.** Rural connectivity in Northern Thailand is unreliable, and a dashboard that shows a blank screen when the internet drops will be abandoned. Cache the last 7 days of data locally and design all chart components to render from the local SQLite store. Test offline mode rigorously: disable network access on a test device and verify that every screen still displays meaningful cached data.

4. **Use the Farm Health Score as the primary engagement hook.** A single number (0–100) that summarizes farm status is the most powerful tool for daily engagement. Farmers will check their score the way they check the weather or stock prices. Make the score prominent on the home screen, include it in LINE bot summaries, and show a 30-day trend line to motivate improvement. Gamification elements (badges for maintaining 90+ scores, comparisons with neighboring farms) can further boost engagement.

5. **Design for one-handed operation.** Farmers often check the dashboard while walking through the coffee plot, holding tools or a cup of coffee in one hand. Place all critical actions and navigation elements in the bottom third of the screen (within thumb reach). Use large touch targets. Avoid gestures that require two fingers (except pinch-to-zoom on detail charts). The "Quick Actions" row at the bottom of the home screen is the most tapped area and must be accessible without stretching.

6. **Localize all units to Thai agricultural conventions.** Use ไร่ (rai) and งาน (ngan) for land area, not hectares or acres. Use millimeters for rainfall and liters for irrigation volume. Display temperature in Celsius only. Use the Buddhist calendar (พ.ศ.) as an option alongside Gregorian. All of these may seem minor but they signal respect for local knowledge and dramatically improve comprehension and trust.

7. **Plan for low-literacy users with icon-first design.** Not all farmers are comfortable reading text, especially technical terms. Every action and status should have a distinct, intuitive icon as its primary identifier, with text as a secondary label. Use universally recognized symbols (water droplet for moisture, thermometer for temperature, shield for disease risk) and test icon comprehension with farmers. Avoid abstract or culturally specific icons that may not translate.
