# Cultivation Best Practices

> **Summary**: Comprehensive guide to coffee cultivation best practices for Northern Thailand, covering planting density and spacing, nursery management, field establishment, pruning systems, shade management, weed control, intercropping, and irrigation — with specific data, rai-based measurements, and hill tribe farming context.

---

## Overview

Cultivation practices account for approximately 20% of the final cup quality in coffee, sitting between variety selection (30%) and the combined influence of harvesting (25%) and post-harvest processing (25%). While genetics set the ceiling for quality potential, cultivation determines whether that ceiling is reached. A poorly cultivated Chiang Mai 80 or Geisha will never express its full cup profile regardless of how carefully it is picked and processed. Conversely, well-cultivated Catimor can achieve specialty-grade scores above 86 points, as demonstrated at Thailand's inaugural Cup of Excellence in 2025.

In Northern Thailand, cultivation practices must contend with unique challenges: steep highland terrain with slopes of 30-60%, acidic laterite and red loam soils, intense tropical radiation exceeding 100,000 lux at midday, a pronounced dry season from November through April, and the ever-present threat of coffee leaf rust (CLR, *Hemileia vastatrix*). The hill tribe farming systems of the Akha, Lisu, Hmong, Karen, Lahu, and Yao communities have developed agroforestry approaches over decades that integrate coffee with miang tea, fruit trees, and subsistence crops. The Royal Project Foundation and Doi Tung Development Project have provided technical frameworks that blend traditional knowledge with modern agronomy, making Northern Thailand one of Southeast Asia's most interesting case studies in sustainable coffee cultivation.

| Quality Factor | Contribution | Key Practices |
|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Variety selection** | 30% | [[Arabica-for-North-Thailand]], [[Robusta-for-North-Thailand]] |
| **Cultivation** | **20%** | Planting density, pruning, shade, nutrition, weed management |
| **Harvesting** | 25% | [[Harvesting-Techniques]], selective picking, ripeness |
| **Post-harvest processing** | 25% | Fermentation, drying, storage |

## Planting Density and Spacing

Planting density is one of the most consequential decisions a coffee farmer makes, affecting yield per rai, bean quality, disease pressure, and long-term plantation health. In Northern Thailand, where smallholder plots average 5-15 rai (0.8-2.4 hectares), optimizing density is critical for economic viability.

### Arabica Spacing

| Spacing (m x m) | Trees/Rai | Trees/Hectare | System Type | Typical Use in N. Thailand |
|------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **1.5 x 1.5** | 444 | 4,444 | High-density hedge | Intensive farms, low-vigor compact varieties |
| **2.0 x 1.5** | 333 | 3,333 | Hedge row | Common compromise; good with Caturra-types |
| **2.0 x 2.0** | 250 | 2,500 | Standard close | Most common for Chiang Mai 80 |
| **2.5 x 2.5** | 256 | 1,600 | Standard | Easier management; better airflow |
| **3.0 x 3.0** | 177 | 1,111 | Traditional wide | Shade-grown, older plantings, Typica/Geisha |
| **3.0 x 2.0** | 266 | 1,667 | Hedge row | Popular compromise for Catimor |

### Robusta Spacing

| Spacing (m x m) | Trees/Rai | Trees/Hectare | System Type | Typical Use |
|------------------|-----------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| **2.0 x 2.0** | 250 | 2,500 | Close planting | Young vigorous clones |
| **2.5 x 2.5** | 256 | 1,600 | Standard | Common for grafted Robusta |
| **3.0 x 3.0** | 177 | 1,111 | Traditional | Seedling Robusta, multiple-stem |
| **4.0 x 4.0** | 100 | 625 | Wide spacing | Large-canopy clones, intercropped |

### Hedge Row System

The hedge row system (also called "close row" or "single row") uses narrow between-plant spacing within rows (1.0-1.5m) with wider between-row spacing (2.5-3.5m) to allow access paths. This system is gaining popularity in Northern Thailand because it maximizes yield per rai while maintaining management access on steep terrain. Typical configurations include 3.0m between rows and 1.5m within rows (approximately 355 trees/rai), or 2.5m between rows and 1.0m within rows (approximately 640 trees/rai for ultra-high density).

### Effects of Density on Yield, Quality, and Disease

| Factor | Low Density (177 trees/rai) | Medium Density (250-300 trees/rai) | High Density (400+ trees/rai) |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Yield per rai** | Lower (120-180 kg) | Moderate (180-280 kg) | Higher initially (250-350 kg) |
| **Yield per tree** | Higher (0.8-1.5 kg) | Moderate (0.6-1.0 kg) | Lower (0.4-0.7 kg) |
| **Bean size** | Larger (screen 16-18) | Good (screen 15-17) | Smaller (screen 13-15) |
| **Cup quality** | Best potential | Good balance | Lower; stress-induced defects |
| **CLR risk** | Lower (good airflow) | Moderate | Higher (humidity, crowding) |
| **CBB pressure** | Lower | Moderate | Higher (more cherries, denser canopy) |
| **Labor efficiency** | Lower per rai | Good | Higher per rai |
| **Lifespan** | 20-30 years | 15-25 years | 10-15 years (stress-related decline) |

**Northern Thailand recommendation**: For most smallholders growing Chiang Mai 80, a spacing of 2.0m x 2.0m (250 trees/rai) or 2.5m x 2.0m (320 trees/rai) provides the best balance of yield, quality, and disease management. For Geisha at higher elevations, wider spacing of 3.0m x 2.5m (213 trees/rai) allows the larger canopy to develop fully.

## Nursery and Seedling Management

Quality seedlings are the foundation of a productive coffee plantation. Poor nursery practices lead to stunted root systems, disease susceptibility, and uneven establishment that plagues the plantation for its entire productive life.

### Seed Selection

Select seeds from healthy, high-yielding mother trees of known variety. For Northern Thailand, the Department of Agriculture (DOA) maintains certified seed gardens at the Chiang Mai Horticultural Research Center and the Royal Project Foundation stations. Seeds should be harvested from fully ripe cherries (red stage), depulped immediately, and processed by fermentation in water for 24-36 hours to remove mucilage. Only seeds that sink in water (dense seeds) should be retained for sowing; floaters indicate hollow or defective beans with low germination potential.

| Seed Parameter | Specification |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Source** | DOA-certified seed gardens, Royal Project nurseries |
| **Cherry ripeness** | Fully red; Brix above 16 |
| **Germination rate** | Minimum 85% (DOA standard) |
| **Seed viability** | 3-6 months from harvest; decreases rapidly after 3 months |
| **Storage** | Moist (30-40% moisture content), in shade, in breathable bags |
| **Seed weight (Arabica)** | Approximately 120-150 seeds per 100g |
| **Seed weight (Robusta)** | Approximately 80-100 seeds per 100g |

### Germination

Arabica seeds germinate in 30-60 days under optimal conditions. Pre-germination in moist sand beds at 25-28 degrees C is the standard practice in Northern Thailand. Seeds are sown 1-2cm deep in sand or fine soil, kept consistently moist but not waterlogged, and shaded at 75-80%. Once the radicle emerges (approximately 7-14 days after sowing), seedlings are transplanted into polybags when the cotyledons unfold and the first true leaves begin to appear.

### Polybag vs Bed Nursery

| Parameter | Polybag Nursery | Bed Nursery |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Bag size** | 12 x 25cm or 15 x 30cm (lay-flat) | N/A |
| **Growing medium** | 1:1:1 topsoil:compost:sand | Raised beds with similar mix |
| **Seedling duration** | 6-8 months | 6-10 months |
| **Root quality** | Good if bag is tall; risk of spiraling | Better natural root architecture |
| **Transplant shock** | Lower (root ball intact) | Higher (root disturbance) |
| **Space efficiency** | Moderate (arrange in blocks) | Lower (permanent bed space) |
| **Cost per seedling** | 5-8 THB (bag + media + labor) | 3-5 THB (media + labor only) |
| **Northern Thailand preference** | **Polybag** (dominant method) | Bed (less common, research stations) |

### Shade Requirements for Seedlings

Seedlings require 50-70% shade during the nursery phase, decreasing gradually to harden plants before field planting. Refer to [[Coffee-Sunlight-Requirements]] for detailed PPFD thresholds by growth stage. Shade nets (black or aluminet) are the standard for nurseries in Northern Thailand, typically mounted on simple bamboo or PVC frames at 1.5-2.0m height. Watering should be done morning and evening, avoiding midday to prevent heat stress and fungal disease.

### Transplanting Timing

The optimal transplanting window aligns with the onset of the rainy season in Northern Thailand, typically late May through June. Soil moisture must be adequate for root establishment before the full force of the rains arrives. Transplanting during the dry season requires irrigation and significantly increases mortality (40-60% loss without irrigation vs. 5-15% with rain-fed establishment). Seedlings should have 4-6 pairs of true leaves and a well-developed taproot (15-20cm) at the time of transplanting.

## Field Establishment and Transplanting

### Land Preparation

Highland coffee plots in Northern Thailand often require terracing or contour planting to control erosion. The Royal Project Foundation promotes contour hedgerow systems with vegetative barriers (Vetiver grass, *Vetiveria zizanioides*, is the standard) planted along the contour lines at 5-10m vertical intervals. Land should be cleared of perennial weeds and debris at least 2-3 months before planting, allowing organic matter to decompose and soil to settle.

| Preparation Step | Timing | Details |
|-----------------|--------|---------|
| **Survey and contour** | 3-4 months before planting | Mark contour lines; plan access paths |
| **Clear vegetation** | 3 months before | Remove perennial weeds; retain useful trees |
| **Terracing (if needed)** | 2-3 months before | Bench terraces or half-moon terraces for steep slopes |
| **Vetiver hedgerows** | 2-3 months before | Plant at 5-10m vertical intervals on contour |
| **Staking** | 1 month before | Mark planting positions with stakes |
| **Hole digging** | 2-4 weeks before | 40 x 40 x 40cm holes; separate topsoil and subsoil |
| **Organic matter** | At planting | Mix 2-3 kg compost + 100g rock phosphate per hole |

### Hole Digging and Soil Amendment

Planting holes should be dug to a minimum of 40 x 40 x 40cm, though 50 x 50 x 50cm is preferred for better root development. In the hard laterite soils common on Northern Thai hillsides, breaking up the subsoil at the base of the hole is essential to allow taproot penetration. Each hole should receive 2-3 kg of well-decomposed compost or aged manure mixed with the topsoil before backfilling. Adding 100-150g of rock phosphate stimulates root development. The backfilled hole should be left to settle for 2-4 weeks before planting.

### Transplanting Technique

Transplanting should be done on a cloudy day or in the late afternoon to reduce transplant shock. The polybag is carefully removed by cutting the bottom and side, the seedling is placed in the center of the prepared hole at the same depth it was in the bag (never deeper, as burying the collar promotes stem rot), and soil is firmed gently around the root ball. A basin or saucer-shaped depression around the seedling helps capture water. Apply 5-10 liters of water immediately after planting.

### Initial Shade Establishment

Temporary shade must be established at the time of transplanting. The most common methods in Northern Thailand include planting banana suckers (3-4 per coffee tree) as fast-growing nurse plants, installing shade nets on bamboo frames, or establishing fast-growing leguminous trees (Gliricidia or Leucaena) planted 6-12 months before the coffee. Banana provides shade within 3-4 months and can be removed after 2-3 years when permanent shade trees mature.

## Pruning Systems

Pruning is arguably the most important yet most neglected cultivation practice in Northern Thailand. Proper pruning maintains plant vigor, manages canopy architecture for light interception and airflow, controls disease, and sustains yield over the plantation's lifespan. Without pruning, coffee trees develop congested canopies that harbor CLR, produce progressively smaller cherries, and become too tall for efficient harvesting.

### Single Stem vs Multiple Stem

| Parameter | Single Stem | Multiple Stem (3-4 stems) |
|-----------|------------|--------------------------|
| **Training** | One main vertical trunk; branches on horizontal plane | 3-4 vertical stems from base |
| **Yield pattern** | Consistent; peaks at years 5-10 | Higher initially; declining after year 8 |
| **Bean quality** | Uniform; consistent ripening | Variable; inner canopy shading |
| **Harvest efficiency** | Easier; accessible canopy | More difficult; dense interior |
| **Disease management** | Better airflow; less CLR | Poorer airflow; higher CLR risk |
| **Suitable for** | High-density planting, mechanized farms | Traditional low-density, Robusta |
| **N. Thailand preference** | **Standard for Arabica** | Common for Robusta and old Arabica |

### Stumping and Rejuvenation Pruning

Stumping (cutting the tree back to 20-30cm above ground) rejuvenates aging plantations and is typically done when yields decline below economic thresholds (below 80 kg/rai for Arabica). After stumping, 2-3 new suckers are selected and trained as replacement stems. First production resumes in 18-24 months, with full yield by year 3 after stumping. In Northern Thailand, stumping is best performed in February-March after harvest, when the plant's carbohydrate reserves are mobilized for new growth before the rainy season.

### Pruning Calendar for Northern Thailand

| Month | Pruning Activity | Details |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **November** | Post-harvest assessment | Evaluate tree condition; mark trees for stumping |
| **December** | Sanitation pruning begins | Remove dead, diseased, and broken branches |
| **January** | Major structural pruning | Shape canopy; remove water sprouts; reduce height |
| **February** | Stumping (if needed) | Cut declining trees to 20-30cm; select 2-3 new suckers by April |
| **March** | Shade tree pruning | Prune Erythrina, Gliricidia before leaf flush; use cuttings as mulch |
| **April** | Sucker management | Remove excess suckers from stumped trees; retain 2-3 strongest |
| **May** | Light maintenance | Remove any new water sprouts before flowering |
| **June-September** | Minimal pruning | Remove diseased branches only (CLR-infected); avoid heavy pruning during rains |
| **October** | Pre-harvest cleanup | Remove low-hanging branches touching soil; sanitation for CBB control |

### Sucker Management

Suckers (vertical shoots from the base or along the main stem) compete with productive branches for nutrients and light. They should be removed promptly when young and soft, as leaving them diverts resources from fruit production. In Northern Thailand, sucker removal is typically done monthly during the growing season (May-October). Suckers from below the graft union on grafted Robusta must be removed immediately, as they are rootstock growth and will dominate the scion.

### Topping

Topping (cutting the main vertical stem at a set height) controls tree height and encourages lateral branching. For Arabica in Northern Thailand, the standard topping height is 1.5-1.8m (single stem) or 1.2-1.5m for each stem in a multiple-stem system. Topping is performed when the young tree reaches the target height, typically 12-18 months after field planting. This stimulates the development of 3-5 primary lateral branches that form the productive framework of the tree.

## Shade Management

Shade management is inseparable from quality coffee production in Northern Thailand's tropical highlands. The region's intense solar radiation and high temperatures make shade essential for Arabica, which evolved as an understory shrub. For detailed light measurements, PPFD thresholds, and shade-species data, see [[Coffee-Sunlight-Requirements]].

### Multi-Strata Shade Systems

The Royal Project Foundation's agroforestry model uses a multi-strata system with four vertical layers, each contributing a different percentage of total shade and providing distinct ecological and economic functions.

| Layer | Height | Species Examples | Shade Contribution |
|-------|--------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Emergent/Timber** | 15-25m | Grevillea robusta, native dipterocarps | 5-10% |
| **Upper Canopy/Fruit** | 8-15m | Macadamia, Longan, Lychee, Peach, Avocado | 20-30% |
| **Sub-Canopy/Legume** | 4-8m | Erythrina, Gliricidia, Leucaena, Inga | 10-20% |
| **Coffee** | 1.5-3m | Arabica (primary crop) | -- |
| **Ground Cover** | 0-0.5m | Arachis pintoi, leaf litter, mulch | -- |

### Shade Percentages by Growth Stage

| Growth Stage | Shade % | Light at Canopy (PPFD) | Implementation |
|-------------|---------|----------------------|----------------|
| **Seedlings (0-6 months)** | 70% | 300-600 umol/m2/s | Shade nets; bamboo shade frames |
| **Young trees (1-2 years)** | 50-60% | 500-900 umol/m2/s | Banana nurse plants; young legumes |
| **First production (3-4 years)** | 40-50% | 600-1,000 umol/m2/s | Maturing shade trees; annual pruning |
| **Mature producing (5+ years)** | 40-50% | 600-1,000 umol/m2/s | Full multi-strata system; regulated by pruning |

### Shade Tree Spacing and Management

Shade trees are planted at 8-12m spacing within the coffee field, yielding approximately 11-25 shade trees per rai (70-150 per hectare). Leguminous trees should be pruned annually in February-March (late dry season), before the rainy season flush. Pruning serves three purposes: it provides leaf mulch and green manure (each mature Gliricidia tree yields 15-30 kg of green leaf biomass per pruning), it regulates shade to the target percentage, and it prevents the shade canopy from becoming too dense and promoting CLR. Fruit trees require less frequent pruning but should be shaped to allow adequate light penetration through the canopy during the coffee flowering period.

## Weed Management

Weed management in Northern Thailand's coffee farms must balance the need to reduce competition for water and nutrients with the imperative to protect soil on steep slopes from erosion. Bare soil on hillsides is a recipe for landslides and nutrient loss during the heavy rains of the monsoon season.

### Manual Weeding vs Herbicide

Manual weeding (using a hoe or hand-pulling) remains the standard practice recommended by the Royal Project Foundation and DOAE. Chemical herbicides, particularly glyphosate (Roundup), are strongly discouraged for several reasons: they damage coffee root systems through soil contact, kill beneficial ground cover and mycorrhizal fungi, contaminate waterways in the watershed, and pose food safety concerns that can disqualify coffee from specialty and organic certification programs. Several Thai specialty coffee buyers now require glyphosate-free certification, testing for residues at the mill.

| Method | Cost (per rai per year) | Effectiveness | Environmental Impact | Certification Impact |
|--------|------------------------|---------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Manual weeding** | 600-1,200 THB (labor) | Good; selective | Minimal | Organic-compatible |
| **Glyphosate** | 100-200 THB | Excellent; non-selective | Severe; soil/water contamination | Disqualifies organic; some specialty buyers reject |
| **Grass-selective herbicide** | 150-300 THB | Moderate; grasses only | Lower than glyphosate | Some certifications allow with restrictions |
| **Cover crops** | 300-500 THB (establishment) | Good; suppresses weeds | Positive; soil improvement | Organic-compatible |

### Ground Cover Crops

| Species | Thai Name | Type | Establishment | Benefits |
|---------|----------|------|--------------|----------|
| **Arachis pintoi** | Thua lap sin | Perennial legume | Seed or rooted cuttings; 3-4 months to cover | N-fixation; erosion control; tolerates shade |
| **Mimosa invisa** | Ya mai yom | Annual/semi-perennial | Direct seed at onset of rains | Fast cover; N-fixation; self-seeding |
| **Calopogonium mucunoides** | -- | Annual/short-lived perennial | Direct seed | Fast initial cover; good for new plantings |
| **Vetiver grass** | Ya faek | Perennial | Slips at 10-15cm spacing on contour | Erosion control on contours; not a ground cover but a hedgerow |

### Mulching

Mulching with coffee parchment, pruned shade tree leaves, rice husks, or dried grass reduces soil temperature by 2-5 degrees C, conserves soil moisture (reducing evaporation by 30-50%), suppresses weed germination, and adds organic matter as it decomposes. A mulch layer of 5-10cm depth around the base of each coffee tree (avoiding direct contact with the stem to prevent collar rot) is recommended. The Royal Project distributes rice husks and composted organic matter to member farmers at subsidized rates of approximately 200-400 THB per rai.

### Weeding Timing

| Season | Weeding Frequency | Priority |
|--------|------------------|----------|
| **Early rainy (May-July)** | Every 3-4 weeks | Critical; weed growth peaks with rains |
| **Mid-rainy (August-September)** | Every 4-6 weeks | Maintain control; avoid seed set |
| **Late rainy (October)** | Once; pre-harvest cleanup | Remove weeds that harbor CBB |
| **Cool/dry (November-February)** | Every 6-8 weeks | Lower growth rate; less critical |
| **Hot/dry (March-April)** | As needed; conserve mulch | Minimal weed pressure; focus on mulch retention |

## Intercropping

Intercropping is not merely a practice in Northern Thailand — it is the dominant farming system. Most hill tribe coffee farms are agroforestry systems by design, integrating multiple crops for food security, income diversification, and ecological resilience. The Royal Project Foundation has actively promoted intercropping models since the 1970s as part of the opium replacement strategy.

### Coffee-Fruit Tree Intercropping

The most successful model is coffee planted under longan, lychee, or macadamia, where the fruit trees serve as both shade and a secondary income stream. At Doi Tung, Arabica coffee under lychee has been the standard system since the 1990s, producing both premium Arabica and export-quality lychee. A typical farm might earn 100,000-250,000 THB per rai from coffee (at specialty prices of 80-150 THB/kg green bean) plus 40,000-120,000 THB per rai from longan, creating a diversified income that reduces dependency on any single market.

### Coffee with Miang Tea

The traditional integration of coffee with miang (fermented tea, *Camellia sinensis* var. *assamica*) is unique to Northern Thailand. Miang tea has been cultivated in the region for centuries, long before coffee was introduced. The two crops are complementary: miang provides additional shade for young coffee, and coffee provides income during the months when tea prices are low. Farmers can shift their labor between the two crops depending on market conditions.

### Other Intercropping Systems

| Intercrop | Spacing with Coffee | Income (THB/rai/year) | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|-------|
| **Black pepper** | 3-4m from coffee tree; trained on Gliricidia posts | 15,000-40,000 | High value; needs support trees; synergizes with shade legumes |
| **Vegetables (cabbage, bean, chili)** | Between coffee rows; seasonal | 10,000-30,000 | Royal Project provides seed and market access; rotated seasonally |
| **Banana** | 3-4m from coffee; temporary | 5,000-15,000 | Nurse plant; removed after 2-3 years; fruit income in interim |
| **Ginger/turmeric** | Between coffee rows; partial shade | 8,000-25,000 | Shade-tolerant; Royal Project promotes for highland areas |

### Royal Project Agroforestry Models

The Royal Project Foundation operates several demonstration agroforestry models across its research stations:

| Model | Layers | Location | Target |
|-------|--------|----------|--------|
| **Model A: Coffee-Lychee-Miang** | Coffee + Lychee + Miang tea | Doi Tung, Chiang Rai | Full income diversification |
| **Model B: Coffee-Macadamia-Legume** | Coffee + Macadamia + Gliricidia | Doi Inthanon, Chiang Mai | Premium nut + coffee; soil improvement |
| **Model C: Coffee-Fruit-Vegetable** | Coffee + Peach/Avocado + seasonal vegetables | Mae Hong Son, Nan | Food security + market crops |
| **Model D: Coffee-Pepper-Legume** | Coffee + Black pepper + Erythrina | Chiang Mai highlands | Maximum per-rai revenue from spice crop |

## Irrigation

### Rainfed vs Supplemental Irrigation

The vast majority of Northern Thailand's Arabica coffee is rainfed, relying on the 1,200-2,500mm annual rainfall that falls predominantly between May and October. However, the 4-5 month dry season (November-April) coincides with critical growth stages including flower bud development, flowering (triggered by first significant rains, typically March-April), and early cherry development. Water stress during these periods can reduce yield by 30-50% and significantly impact bean quality.

### Critical Irrigation Periods

| Growth Stage | Timing | Water Requirement | Impact of Deficit |
|-------------|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Flower bud development** | February-April | 30-50mm/week | Poor flower initiation; reduced cherry set |
| **Flowering** | March-May (first rains) | Adequate soil moisture essential | Flower abortion; uneven fruit set |
| **Cherry expansion** | May-August | 40-60mm/week | Small cherries; low bean weight |
| **Cherry maturation** | September-November | 20-30mm/week | Uneven ripening; low Brix |
| **Post-harvest recovery** | December-February | 15-25mm/week | Vegetative stress; reduced next-year bearing |

### Drip Irrigation

Drip irrigation is the most water-efficient method for highland coffee, delivering 2-4 liters per tree per day during the dry season. A basic drip system for 1 rai (177-250 trees) costs approximately 8,000-15,000 THB for materials and installation, with an operational cost of approximately 500-1,000 THB per year for maintenance. The Royal Project Foundation has piloted drip irrigation at several stations, achieving 20-35% yield increases over rainfed controls during drought years. Water sources in the highlands include mountain streams, reservoirs, and rainwater collection tanks.

| Irrigation Method | Cost (THB/rai) | Water Use (L/tree/day) | Yield Impact | Suitability |
|-------------------|----------------|----------------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Rainfed** | 0 | 0 (natural) | Baseline | Standard for most farms |
| **Drip** | 8,000-15,000 | 2-4 | +20-35% in dry years | Best ROI; water-efficient |
| **Sprinkler** | 10,000-20,000 | 6-12 | +15-25% | Less efficient; increases CLR humidity |
| **Furrow** | 2,000-5,000 | 8-15 | +10-20% | Not suitable for steep terrain |

## Northern Thailand Context

The cultivation practices described in this document are shaped by the specific conditions of Northern Thailand's highland coffee zone. The four key coffee-producing provinces — Chiang Mai, Chiang Rai, Mae Hong Son, and Nan — share a mountainous topography with narrow valleys and steep ridges that define every aspect of farm management. Terracing and contour planting are not optional; they are essential for soil conservation on slopes that regularly exceed 30 degrees. The hill tribe communities that form the backbone of the region's coffee labor force bring generations of agroforestry knowledge but may have limited access to technical training and inputs.

The Royal Project Foundation, established by King Bhumibol Adulyadej in 1969, provides the institutional infrastructure that makes coffee cultivation viable in these remote highlands. Its network of research stations, extension officers, nurseries, and buying stations supports an estimated 30,000+ farming households across the region. The Doi Tung Development Project (established 1988 by Princess Srinagarindra) adds another layer of support in Chiang Rai province, with a vertically integrated model from seedling production to roasted coffee.

Land tenure remains a challenge. Many hill tribe farmers operate on national forest reserve land without formal title, creating uncertainty that discourages long-term investment in shade trees, irrigation, and soil improvement. The Royal Project and government programs have worked to provide usufruct rights and community land titles, but the issue persists. Climate change is amplifying existing stresses: rising temperatures are pushing the optimal Arabica zone upward (an estimated 100m per decade), dry seasons are becoming more pronounced, and unseasonal rainfall during the flowering period disrupts cherry set. The PM2.5 haze from agricultural burning (February-April) reduces photosynthesis through particulate deposition on leaves and may affect flowering physiology.

Despite these challenges, Northern Thailand's coffee sector has significant competitive advantages: a young and innovative farmer population (average age 25-35, the youngest globally), strong institutional support, growing international recognition (EU PGI status for Doi Tung and Doi Chang), and a processing revolution since 2016 that has proven the quality potential of even the humble Catimor variety.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Plant at appropriate density**: Use 2.0 x 2.0m spacing (250 trees/rai) for Chiang Mai 80; 3.0 x 2.5m (213 trees/rai) for Geisha. Avoid ultra-high density on steep terrain where airflow is already limited.
2. **Invest in quality seedlings**: Source from DOA-certified or Royal Project nurseries. A 5 THB savings on a poor seedling costs thousands of THB in lost yield over the tree's lifespan.
3. **Establish shade before coffee**: Plant Gliricidia, Erythrina, or fruit trees 6-12 months before transplanting coffee. If this is not possible, use banana as temporary nurse plants.
4. **Transplant with the rains**: Target late May to mid-June. Never transplant in the dry season without irrigation infrastructure in place.
5. **Prune every year after harvest**: Follow the pruning calendar strictly. The February-April window is critical for structural pruning, stumping, and shade tree management.
6. **Manage shade to 40-50% for mature Arabica**: Use a spherical densiometer or smartphone shade app to measure canopy cover. Prune shade trees annually in February-March.
7. **Avoid glyphosate**: Use manual weeding and establish Arachis pintoi as a living ground cover. This protects certification eligibility and soil health.
8. **Intercrop for income diversification**: Integrate longan, lychee, macadamia, or black pepper into the shade system. The additional income often exceeds the coffee income per rai.
9. **Install drip irrigation if affordable**: Even supplemental irrigation during the February-April dry period can increase yield by 20-35% and protect against drought-induced quality loss.
10. **Maintain detailed records**: Track planting dates, pruning schedules, shade percentages, and yields per rai. Data-driven management is the foundation for continuous improvement.
11. **Participate in extension programs**: The Royal Project Foundation, DOAE, and specialty coffee associations offer training, subsidized inputs, and market access. Active participation correlates strongly with farm profitability.
12. **Stump aging trees on a 15-year cycle**: Don't wait for yields to crash. Proactive stumping at year 12-15 maintains plantation productivity and provides an opportunity to introduce improved varieties.
