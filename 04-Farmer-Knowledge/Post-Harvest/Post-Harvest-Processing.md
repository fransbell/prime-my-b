# Post-Harvest Processing

> **Summary**: Comprehensive guide to coffee post-harvest processing methods for Northern Thailand, covering traditional wet, dry, and honey processes alongside the 2016 Thai processing revolution (SCM, yeast inoculation, anaerobic fermentation), fermentation science, drying protocols, hulling, grading, storage, and IoT-enabled monitoring for quality optimization.

---

## Overview

Post-harvest processing is the critical transformation stage that converts freshly harvested coffee cherries into stable, storable green beans — and it accounts for approximately 25% of the final cup quality. While variety selection determines roughly 30% and cultivation and harvesting each contribute around 20-25%, the choices made during processing can elevate modest Catimor to specialty grade or ruin exceptional Geisha through careless handling. In Northern Thailand, the significance of post-harvest processing was dramatically demonstrated by the 2016 Thai processing revolution, when innovative fermentation methods proved that Chiang Mai 80 (Catimor) — long dismissed as a commodity variety — could achieve SCA scores of 84+ and even reach Cup of Excellence caliber at 86.73 points (COE 2025, Karen Farm Chomthong).

Before 2016, the vast majority of Northern Thai Arabica was processed using simple washed or natural methods with minimal fermentation control. A cadre of Thai researchers, most notably at Rajamangala University of Technology Lanna (RMUTL) in Chiang Mai, began experimenting with controlled fermentation environments inspired by winemaking. The Brixter Process, Semi-Carbonic Maceration (SCM), and Yeast Inoculation methods they developed have since been adopted by leading specialty producers across the region, transforming Northern Thailand from a commodity Arabica origin into a recognized specialty coffee destination.

The integration of IoT monitoring into post-harvest processing represents the next frontier. Temperature sensors in fermentation tanks, humidity sensors in drying facilities, and moisture meters with data logging allow farmers to maintain precise control over processing parameters that were previously managed by intuition alone. For a region where smallholder farmers process 500-5,000 kg of cherry per season, even modest quality improvements translate directly into significant income gains — the difference between 80 THB/kg commodity price and 280-1,000+ THB/kg specialty price.

| Quality Determinant | Contribution | Key Factors |
|---------------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Variety / Genetics** | ~30% | Catimor, Typica, Geisha; altitude adaptation |
| **Cultivation** | ~20% | Soil health, shade, fertilization, pruning |
| **Harvesting** | ~25% | Cherry ripeness, selective picking, timing |
| **Post-Harvest Processing** | ~25% | Method, fermentation, drying, storage |

## Wet Process (Washed)

### Overview

The wet process, also known as the washed process, is the most widely used method for Arabica in Northern Thailand and is considered the standard for specialty-grade coffee. It produces a clean, bright cup that clearly expresses the inherent qualities of the variety and terroir. The method requires significant water and infrastructure investment but yields the most consistent and predictable results, making it the recommended starting point for farmers transitioning to specialty production.

### Detailed Steps

**1. Float Tank Separation**

Freshly harvested cherries are poured into a tank of water. Ripe, dense cherries sink while under-ripe, over-ripe, and insect-damaged cherries float. The floaters are skimmed off and processed separately or discarded. This simple step can improve cup quality by 1-2 SCA points by removing defective cherries before processing begins. In Northern Thailand, where harvesting labor is often family-based and selective picking is imperfect, float tank separation is an essential quality gate.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Water temperature** | Ambient (15-25 degrees C in Northern Thai highlands) |
| **Soak duration** | 5-15 minutes |
| **Defect removal rate** | 5-15% of total cherry volume |
| **Tank cost** | 500-3,000 THB (concrete or stainless steel) |

**2. Depulping**

Sinking cherries are passed through a mechanical depulper that squeezes the outer skin and most of the mucilage off the seed. Two main depulper types are used in Northern Thailand:

| Depulper Type | Capacity | Cost (THB) | Notes |
|---------------|----------|------------|-------|
| **Hand-crank disc depulper** | 50-100 kg/hr | 3,000-5,000 | Entry-level; suitable for smallholders |
| **Motorized disc depulper** | 200-500 kg/hr | 8,000-15,000 | Standard for community stations |
| **Drum depulper** | 300-800 kg/hr | 12,000-25,000 | Gentler processing; less damage to beans |

The depulper must be calibrated correctly — too tight and beans are crushed, too loose and mucilage remains. A well-calibrated depulper removes approximately 90% of the mucilage, leaving a thin sticky layer that is removed during fermentation.

**3. Fermentation (12-36 hours)**

Depulped beans, still coated in mucilage, are placed in fermentation tanks filled with water (wet fermentation) or left in piles without water (dry fermentation). Natural microorganisms — primarily yeasts and bacteria present on the cherry surface — break down the mucilage's pectin layer through enzymatic action. The fermentation is complete when the mucilage feels gritty rather than slimy when a handful of beans is rubbed together. In Northern Thailand's cool highland climate (15-22 degrees C during harvest season, November-February), fermentation takes longer than in tropical lowlands — typically 18-36 hours versus 12-18 hours in warmer regions.

| Fermentation Parameter | Value |
|------------------------|-------|
| **Duration (wet fermentation)** | 12-24 hours (warm); 18-36 hours (cool highland) |
| **Duration (dry fermentation)** | 12-24 hours (warm); 18-30 hours (cool highland) |
| **Optimal temperature** | 20-30 degrees C |
| **Target pH endpoint** | 4.2-4.5 |
| **Tank material** | Concrete, stainless steel, or food-grade plastic |
| **Tank cost** | 2,000-10,000 THB (varies by size and material) |

**4. Washing**

After fermentation, beans are agitated and washed in channels or tanks to remove the loosened mucilage. This is repeated until the water runs clear and beans feel clean to the touch. Washing channels with gently flowing water are traditional, but recirculating wash systems are increasingly adopted to reduce water consumption.

**5. Soaking (Optional)**

Some producers soak washed beans in clean water for an additional 12-24 hours. This step, common in Kenyan and Colombian processing, is believed to improve cup cleanliness and brightness by removing residual fermentation byproducts. Soaking is gaining adoption among Northern Thai specialty producers.

**6. Drying**

Washed parchment coffee is dried to a target moisture content of 10-12%. See the [[#Drying]] section for detailed drying protocols.

### Water Usage

Water consumption is a significant concern for wet processing, particularly in highland communities where water access can be seasonal. Standard wet processing uses 5-40 liters of water per kilogram of cherry, with the wide range reflecting differences in equipment and practices.

| Practice | Water Usage (L/kg cherry) | Notes |
|----------|---------------------------|-------|
| **Traditional washing** | 20-40 | Multiple wash cycles; flowing water |
| **Efficient washing** | 10-20 | Recirculating systems; controlled flow |
| **Eco-pulper + minimal wash** | 3-5 | Mechanical mucilage removal reduces water |
| **Dry fermentation** | 5-10 | No water during fermentation; wash only |

### Quality Characteristics

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cup clarity** | High — clean, transparent flavor |
| **Acidity** | Bright, well-defined |
| **Body** | Light to medium |
| **Flavor expression** | Variety and terroir forward |
| **SCA score range** | 80-87 (well-processed Arabica) |
| **Market positioning** | Specialty standard; reliable quality |

### When to Use Wet Process

- When water is readily available (streams, springs common in Northern Thai highlands)
- When targeting specialty markets that value cleanliness and acidity
- When processing Chiang Mai 80 / Catimor (highlights inherent chocolate and caramel notes)
- When consistent, predictable quality is the priority
- When selling to exporters who specify washed Arabica

## Dry Process (Natural)

### Overview

The dry process, or natural process, is the oldest method of coffee processing and requires minimal infrastructure — the whole cherry is dried intact on raised beds or patios without water or depulping equipment. In Northern Thailand, the natural process is used for approximately 15-25% of Arabica production and is gaining popularity among specialty producers seeking fruit-forward, complex cup profiles. However, the method carries significant risks of over-fermentation and mold, making it unsuitable for humid or rainy conditions.

### Detailed Steps

**1. Cherry Sorting**

Because the entire cherry is dried, any defects present will be amplified in the cup. Rigorous sorting is essential — float tank separation followed by hand-sorting to remove under-ripe, over-ripe, and damaged cherries. This step is more critical for natural process than for any other method.

**2. Drying (2-4 weeks)**

Sorted cherries are spread in thin layers (2-5 cm depth) on raised beds or patios. The drying process is significantly longer than for washed coffee because the entire fruit must desiccate, not just the parchment layer. Cherries must be turned frequently — every 30-60 minutes during the first week — to ensure even drying and prevent localized fermentation or mold growth. As drying progresses, the cherries darken from red to deep purple-brown and eventually to a hard, dark brown shell.

| Drying Parameter | Value |
|------------------|-------|
| **Duration** | 14-28 days (sun-drying); 10-20 days (hybrid sun/mechanical) |
| **Initial layer depth** | 2-3 cm |
| **Turning frequency (week 1)** | Every 30-60 minutes |
| **Turning frequency (week 2+)** | Every 2-3 hours |
| **Target moisture content** | 10-12% (dried cherry) |
| **Risk temperature** | Above 35 degrees C (case hardening) |

**3. Hulling**

Once fully dried, the dried cherry (now including skin, dried mucilage, and parchment) is hulled in a single step to remove all outer layers. This requires a heavier-duty huller than parchment hulling.

### Quality Characteristics

| Attribute | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Body** | Full, syrupy |
| **Acidity** | Lower, rounded |
| **Flavor** | Fruit-forward; strawberry, blueberry, tropical fruit |
| **Sweetness** | High — natural sugars concentrate during drying |
| **Complexity** | High — but includes risk of fermented/off flavors |
| **SCA score range** | 78-88 (wide range due to variability) |

### Risks and Challenges

| Risk | Cause | Mitigation |
|------|-------|------------|
| **Over-fermentation** | Uneven drying; delayed turning | Frequent turning; thin layers; immediate drying after harvest |
| **Mold** | High humidity; rain during drying | Covered raised beds; rapid relocation during rain |
| **Inconsistent quality** | Uneven ripeness amplified | Rigorous sorting before drying |
| **Weather dependency** | Requires 14+ consecutive dry days | Northern Thailand dry season (Nov-Feb) is ideal |
| **Case hardening** | Drying too fast at high temperature | Shade cloth; avoid midday direct sun in early stages |

### Weather Dependency

The natural process is ideally suited to Northern Thailand's dry season (November-February), when daytime temperatures of 22-30 degrees C, low humidity (40-60%), and minimal rainfall create optimal drying conditions. Farmers attempting natural process during the early or late harvest season face significantly higher risk of rain-induced defects. The coolider highland temperatures are actually an advantage, slowing drying slightly and allowing more even moisture removal compared to lowland tropical environments.

## Honey Process (Pulped Natural)

### Overview

The honey process, also known as pulped natural, occupies a middle ground between washed and natural processing. Cherries are depulped but the sticky mucilage layer is intentionally left on the parchment during drying, producing a cup with more body and sweetness than washed coffee but greater cleanliness than natural. The name "honey" refers to the sticky, honey-like appearance of the mucilage — not to any honey flavor. This method has gained significant traction in Northern Thailand as it requires less water than full washing and can produce excellent specialty results with moderate infrastructure investment.

### Honey Process Variants by Mucilage Remaining

| Variant | Mucilage Remaining | Drying Time | Flavor Profile | Complexity |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| **White Honey** | 0-25% | 7-10 days | Clean, bright; closest to washed | Low |
| **Yellow Honey** | 25-50% | 10-14 days | Balanced sweetness; mild fruit | Low-Medium |
| **Red Honey** | 50-75% | 14-18 days | Sweet, fruity; moderate body | Medium |
| **Black Honey** | 75-100% | 18-25+ days | Full body, complex fruit, high sweetness | High |

### Detailed Steps

**1. Cherry Sorting and Float Tank** — Same as wet process.

**2. Depulping** — Cherries are passed through a depulper. The key difference is that the depulper is calibrated to leave the desired amount of mucilage on the parchment. For white honey, a standard or slightly aggressive depulper setting is used. For black honey, a gentler setting or manual removal of skin only is employed. Some producers use an eco-pulper with adjustable mucilage removal settings.

**3. Drying (No Fermentation Tank)** — The depulped beans with mucilage intact are transferred directly to raised beds. This is the critical distinction: no controlled fermentation occurs. Instead, the mucilage dries onto the parchment, and mild fermentation happens spontaneously during the drying process. The sticky mucilage creates a protective, sugary coating that caramelizes during drying, contributing sweetness and body.

| Drying Parameter | White Honey | Yellow Honey | Red Honey | Black Honey |
|------------------|-------------|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Layer depth** | 3-5 cm | 3-4 cm | 2-3 cm | 2-3 cm |
| **Turning frequency** | Every 1-2 hrs | Every 1-2 hrs | Every 30-60 min | Every 30 min |
| **Shade requirement** | Partial | Partial | More shade | Full shade (first days) |
| **Risk of fermentation** | Low | Low-Medium | Medium | High |
| **Duration** | 7-10 days | 10-14 days | 14-18 days | 18-25+ days |

### Quality Characteristics

| Attribute | White Honey | Yellow Honey | Red Honey | Black Honey |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Body** | Medium | Medium-Full | Full | Very Full |
| **Acidity** | Bright | Moderate | Rounded | Low-Moderate |
| **Sweetness** | Moderate | High | Very High | Intense |
| **Flavor notes** | Clean, floral, citrus | Honey, caramel, mild fruit | Berry, stone fruit, chocolate | Dark fruit, molasses, wine |
| **SCA score range** | 80-85 | 81-86 | 82-87 | 83-88 |
| **Risk level** | Low | Low-Medium | Medium | High |

### Equipment

| Equipment | Cost (THB) | Notes |
|-----------|------------|-------|
| **Depulper (adjustable)** | 8,000-20,000 | Must be adjustable for mucilage control |
| **Eco-pulper** | 25,000-50,000 | Precise mucilage removal; low water |
| **Raised beds** | 500-2,000 per bed | Bamboo or metal frame; mesh bottom |
| **Shade cloth** | 200-500 per sq meter | Essential for black and red honey |

## Innovative Processing Methods

### The 2016 Thai Processing Revolution

The year 2016 marked a watershed moment for Thai specialty coffee. A group of researchers and processors in Northern Thailand, inspired by winemaking and craft beer fermentation science, began applying controlled fermentation techniques to coffee processing. The results were transformative — Catimor lots that had previously scored 76-80 SCA points suddenly reached 84-87 points, with dramatically improved complexity, acidity, and flavor clarity. These methods have since been refined and are now used by leading Thai specialty producers and cooperatives.

### Semi-Carbonic Maceration (SCM)

Semi-Carbonic Maceration adapts the winemaking technique of carbonic maceration to coffee. Depulped or whole cherries are placed in a sealed tank, and CO2 is injected to displace oxygen, creating an anaerobic environment. The intracellular fermentation that occurs within the beans under CO2 atmosphere produces distinctive fruity, wine-like flavors and enhanced complexity.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Cherry state** | Whole cherry or depulped |
| **Tank** | Stainless steel or food-grade plastic, sealed with one-way valve |
| **CO2 source** | Compressed gas cylinder or fermentation-generated |
| **CO2 pressure** | 0.5-1.0 atm (slight positive pressure) |
| **Temperature** | 18-25 degrees C |
| **Duration** | 48-96 hours |
| **pH monitoring** | Target endpoint pH 4.0-4.5 |
| **Post-SCM drying** | Raised bed, 10-18 days |
| **SCA score range** | 84-89 (Catimor); 87-92+ (Geisha) |
| **Equipment cost** | 15,000-80,000 THB (sealed tank + CO2 system) |

### Yeast Inoculation

Yeast inoculation involves introducing specific cultured yeast strains to the fermentation tank, replacing or supplementing the wild microorganisms naturally present on cherry surfaces. Different yeast strains produce different metabolic byproducts, allowing processors to steer flavor development in targeted directions.

| Yeast Strain | Source | Flavor Contribution | Duration | Cost |
|--------------|--------|--------------------|---------|------|
| **Saccharomyces cerevisiae** | Commercial wine/brewing yeast | Clean, bright acidity; enhanced sweetness | 24-72 hrs | 500-1,500 THB per batch |
| **Pichia kluyveri** | Specialty coffee yeast (Lallemand) | Tropical fruit, stone fruit aroma | 48-96 hrs | 1,000-3,000 THB per batch |
| **Torulaspora delbrueckii** | Wine yeast | Complex fruity esters; enhanced body | 48-120 hrs | 1,500-4,000 THB per batch |
| **Wild-ferment blend** | Natural cherry microbiome + starter | Variable; terroir-expressive | 36-72 hrs | 300-800 THB per batch |

The yeast is typically rehydrated and added to the fermentation tank at a concentration of 0.1-0.5 g per kg of cherry. Temperature control is critical — most strains perform optimally between 20-28 degrees C. IoT temperature sensors in fermentation tanks allow real-time monitoring and alerting if temperatures fall outside the target range.

### Coffivino Process

The Coffivino Process is a Thai innovation that processes coffee in wine-like conditions. Cherries are depulped and the mucilage-coated parchment is fermented in a controlled environment with specific yeast strains and temperature profiles modeled on red wine fermentation protocols. The result is a coffee with pronounced wine-like qualities — grape, berry, and fermented fruit notes — that has become a signature of Thai specialty processing.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Cherry state** | Depulped with mucilage intact |
| **Fermentation vessel** | Temperature-controlled stainless steel tank |
| **Yeast** | Saccharomyces cerevisiae (wine strain) |
| **Temperature** | 22-28 degrees C (controlled) |
| **Duration** | 72-120 hours |
| **Punch-down** | Manual agitation every 8-12 hours (like wine cap management) |
| **pH target** | 3.8-4.3 |
| **SCA score range** | 84-88 (Catimor); 86-91 (higher varieties) |
| **Market price** | 350-800 THB/kg (green bean) |

### Brixter Process

The Brixter Process was specifically developed by researchers at Rajamangala University of Technology Lanna (RMUTL) in Chiang Mai. It uses controlled Brix measurement of the cherry juice to determine optimal harvest timing and processing parameters. Cherries are sorted by Brix level (measured with a refractometer), and each Brix tier is processed separately using customized fermentation profiles. This approach recognizes that sugar content directly drives fermentation dynamics, and that uniform Brix levels produce more consistent and controllable results.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Brix measurement** | Refractometer; 15-23 Brix range for specialty |
| **Cherry sorting** | By Brix tier: 15-17, 18-20, 21-23 |
| **Fermentation** | Customized by Brix tier; higher Brix = longer fermentation |
| **Temperature** | 20-26 degrees C (controlled) |
| **Duration** | 36-96 hours (varies by Brix tier) |
| **Developed by** | RMUTL, Chiang Mai |
| **Refractometer cost** | 1,500-5,000 THB |
| **SCA score improvement** | +2-5 points over standard washed |

### Anaerobic Fermentation

Anaerobic fermentation involves placing coffee in a sealed container without oxygen and allowing fermentation to proceed under the pressure of naturally produced CO2. Unlike SCM, no external CO2 is injected — the gas is generated by the fermentation process itself. The sealed environment creates unique pressure conditions that affect enzymatic and microbial activity, producing distinctive flavor compounds.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Cherry state** | Whole cherry or depulped |
| **Container** | Sealed stainless steel or food-grade plastic drum |
| **Pressure relief** | One-way valve (releases gas above 0.5-1.0 atm) |
| **Temperature** | 18-28 degrees C |
| **Duration** | 48-120 hours |
| **Pressure monitoring** | Essential; over-pressure risks off-flavors |
| **pH endpoint** | 4.0-4.5 |
| **Equipment cost** | 8,000-30,000 THB |
| **SCA score range** | 83-89 |

### Thermal Shock

Thermal shock processing subjects coffee to rapid temperature changes — typically a hot water treatment followed by cold water — before or after fermentation. The theory is that temperature shock modifies cell wall permeability, allowing different compounds to migrate between the mucilage and the seed during fermentation, and selectively inhibiting or promoting specific microbial populations.

| Parameter | Hot Shock | Cold Shock |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| **Temperature** | 50-70 degrees C | 5-15 degrees C |
| **Duration** | 1-5 minutes | 5-15 minutes |
| **Sequence** | Hot then cold, or cold then hot | — |
| **Effect** | Alters cell permeability; modifies microbial ecology | Locks in compounds; slows fermentation |
| **Equipment** | Temperature-controlled water bath | Ice bath or chilled water system |
| **Cost** | 3,000-10,000 THB | 2,000-8,000 THB |

### Lactic Fermentation

Lactic fermentation promotes the growth of lactic acid bacteria (LAB) over other microorganisms, producing a distinctive creamy, yogurt-like acidity and enhanced body. This is achieved by inoculating with LAB cultures (Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus) or by creating environmental conditions that favor LAB — moderate temperature (25-30 degrees C), slightly acidic pH, and limited oxygen.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Inoculant** | Lactobacillus plantarum, L. brevis, or mixed LAB culture |
| **Temperature** | 25-30 degrees C |
| **Duration** | 48-96 hours |
| **pH endpoint** | 3.8-4.2 |
| **Flavor result** | Creamy, yogurt-like, lactic acidity, enhanced body |
| **SCA score range** | 83-88 |
| **LAB culture cost** | 800-2,500 THB per batch |

## Fermentation Science

### Microbial Ecology

Coffee fermentation is driven by a complex community of microorganisms — yeasts, bacteria, and fungi — that are naturally present on the cherry surface, in the processing environment, and in the water supply. The dominant microbial groups and their roles are:

| Microorganism | Role | Metabolite | Flavor Impact |
|---------------|------|-----------|---------------|
| **Yeasts (Saccharomyces, Pichia, Candida)** | Sugar metabolism; alcohol production | Ethanol, CO2, esters, higher alcohols | Fruity, floral, fermented aromas |
| **Lactic acid bacteria (Lactobacillus, Leuconostoc)** | Sugar and organic acid metabolism | Lactic acid, acetic acid, diacetyl | Creamy, buttery, tangy |
| **Acetic acid bacteria (Acetobacter, Gluconobacter)** | Alcohol oxidation | Acetic acid | Vinegar, sharp; positive in small amounts |
| **Bacillus species** | Pectin degradation | Pectinases, proteases | Variable; risk of off-flavors if dominant |
| **Filamentous fungi (Aspergillus, Penicillium)** | Opportunistic; spoilage organisms | Mycotoxins, off-flavors | Negative; must be controlled |

### Temperature Control

Temperature is the single most important controllable parameter in fermentation. It directly affects microbial growth rates, enzymatic activity, and the balance between different microbial populations. In Northern Thailand's cool highland climate, ambient temperatures during harvest season (November-February) typically range from 10-25 degrees C, which is generally favorable for controlled fermentation but may require warming during cold nights.

| Temperature Range | Effect | Recommendation |
|-------------------|--------|---------------|
| **Below 15 degrees C** | Fermentation very slow; risk of stalling | Warm fermentation area; insulated tanks |
| **15-20 degrees C** | Slow but clean fermentation | Extend duration to 36-48+ hours |
| **20-30 degrees C** | Optimal range | Standard duration; monitor pH |
| **30-35 degrees C** | Fast fermentation; risk of off-flavors | Shorten duration; cool if possible |
| **Above 35 degrees C** | Uncontrolled; high defect risk | Avoid; use shade and ventilation |

IoT temperature sensors placed inside fermentation tanks provide real-time monitoring with data logging, allowing farmers to track temperature curves and make informed decisions about fermentation duration. The [[Temperature-Sensors]] commonly used in IoT agriculture systems (DS18B20, TMP36) can be deployed with waterproof probes in fermentation tanks for 200-500 THB per sensor node.

### pH Monitoring

pH is the most reliable indicator of fermentation progress and endpoint. As fermentation proceeds, organic acids accumulate and pH drops. Over-fermentation occurs when pH drops too low, producing sour, astringent, or fermented off-flavors.

| Fermentation Stage | pH Range | Description |
|--------------------|---------|-------------|
| **Start** | 5.5-6.5 | Fresh cherry; minimal acid |
| **Active** | 4.8-5.5 | Yeast and LAB active; CO2 production |
| **Target endpoint** | 4.2-4.5 | Mucilage loosened; optimal flavor development |
| **Extended** | 3.8-4.2 | Increased complexity; higher risk |
| **Over-fermented** | Below 3.8 | Sour, acetic, defective |

Portable pH meters cost 500-3,000 THB and should be calibrated weekly. IoT pH sensor systems with continuous monitoring and alerting are available for 3,000-15,000 THB and provide the most precise control for innovative processing methods.

### Over-Fermentation Risks

Over-fermentation is the most common and costly defect in Thai coffee processing. It occurs when fermentation continues beyond the optimal endpoint, producing excessive organic acids, alcohols, and undesirable flavor compounds. The risk is highest during warm periods, with unmonitored fermentation, or when drying is delayed after washing. Signs of over-fermentation include vinegar-like odor, mushy bean texture, and a pH below 3.8. Cup defects include sourness, astringency, fermented/off flavors, and loss of sweetness — any of which can drop an SCA score by 5-10 points.

## Drying

### Overview

Drying is the critical post-fermentation step that stabilizes the coffee bean by reducing moisture content to a level where microbial activity and enzymatic reactions cease. Improper drying is one of the most common causes of quality loss in Northern Thailand, where unpredictable rain during the late harvest season (February-March) can ruin entire lots. The target moisture content is 10-12% for parchment coffee and 11-12% for green bean.

### Sun-Drying on Raised Beds (Preferred)

Raised beds with mesh bottoms allow airflow from all sides, producing the most even and gentle drying. This is the preferred method for specialty coffee in Northern Thailand.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Bed construction** | Bamboo or metal frame; nylon or stainless mesh |
| **Bed size** | 1x2m to 1.5x5m (typical) |
| **Loading rate** | 10-15 kg parchment per sq meter |
| **Drying duration** | 7-14 days (washed); 14-28 days (natural) |
| **Target moisture** | 10-12% |
| **Turning frequency** | Every 1-2 hours during active drying |
| **Cost per bed** | 500-2,000 THB |
| **Rain protection** | Plastic sheet or retractable roof; 200-500 THB per sq meter |

### Patio Drying

Concrete or clay patios are less expensive than raised beds but provide only top-surface airflow, resulting in slower and less even drying. Patios are common in larger processing stations but require more frequent turning to prevent mold development on the underside.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Material** | Concrete, clay tile, or brick |
| **Loading rate** | 15-25 kg parchment per sq meter |
| **Drying duration** | 7-18 days (washed) |
| **Turning frequency** | Every 30-60 minutes |
| **Cost** | 500-1,500 THB per sq meter (construction) |
| **Risk** | Higher risk of uneven drying and mold |

### Mechanical Drying (Guardiolas)

Mechanical dryers (guardiolas or rotary drum dryers) provide climate-independent drying but require fuel and careful temperature control. They are used in Northern Thailand primarily as backup during rainy periods or for large-scale operations.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Type** | Guardiola (rotating drum) or batch dryer |
| **Temperature (max)** | 40-45 degrees C — above this causes quality loss |
| **Duration** | 24-72 hours (depending on starting moisture) |
| **Batch size** | 100-2,000 kg parchment |
| **Fuel** | Coffee husk, LPG, or diesel |
| **Equipment cost** | 50,000-300,000 THB |
| **Operating cost** | 2-5 THB per kg parchment |
| **Quality risk** | Bakey, flat flavors if temperature exceeds 45 degrees C |

### Moisture Content Targets and Measurement

| Stage | Target Moisture | Measurement Tool | Cost (THB) |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|------------|
| **End of drying (parchment)** | 10-12% | Pin-type moisture meter | 2,000-8,000 |
| **Before hulling** | 10.5-11.5% | Moisture meter | — |
| **Green bean (ready for sale)** | 11-12% | Moisture meter (calibrated) | — |
| **Storage threshold** | Below 13% (above = mold risk) | Continuous IoT monitoring | 5,000-15,000 |

### Northern Thailand Drying Season

The Northern Thailand dry season (November through February) provides ideal sun-drying conditions: daytime temperatures of 22-30 degrees C, low humidity (40-60%), consistent sunshine, and minimal rainfall. This period coincides with the main Arabica harvest, making sun-drying the most practical and economical method for most producers. Farmers harvesting in October or March face higher rain risk and may need covered drying structures or mechanical drying backup.

The [[Humidity-Sensors]] deployed in drying facilities can monitor ambient humidity and trigger alerts when conditions favor mold growth (above 70% relative humidity for extended periods). Combined with moisture meters, this creates a comprehensive drying monitoring system.

## Hulling and Grading

### Hulling

Hulling removes the dried parchment layer from washed or honey-processed coffee, or the entire dried fruit casing from natural-processed coffee. This step must be performed at the correct moisture content (10.5-11.5%) to avoid bean damage.

| Huller Type | Capacity | Cost (THB) | Notes |
|-------------|----------|------------|-------|
| **Hand-crank huller** | 30-50 kg/hr | 5,000-10,000 | Smallholders; labor-intensive |
| **Motorized friction huller** | 200-500 kg/hr | 15,000-40,000 | Standard for community stations |
| **Catalan huller** | 300-800 kg/hr | 30,000-80,000 | Gentle; low breakage rate |
| **Huller-polisher combination** | 500-1,000 kg/hr | 50,000-120,000 | Integrated hulling and polishing |

## Storage

### Parchment vs Green Bean Storage

Coffee can be stored either as parchment (with the dried parchment layer intact) or as hulled green bean. Parchment storage is preferred for longer-term storage as the parchment layer provides natural protection against moisture and environmental odors.

| Parameter | Parchment Storage | Green Bean Storage |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Shelf life** | 12-18 months | 6-12 months |
| **Moisture stability** | Better — parchment buffers | More vulnerable |
| **Space requirement** | Higher (parchment adds volume) | Lower |
| **Quality preservation** | Superior for long storage | Acceptable for short-term |
| **Transport cost** | Higher (extra weight) | Lower |

### Warehouse Conditions

| Parameter | Target | Critical Threshold | Monitoring Tool |
|-----------|--------|--------------------|----------------|
| **Temperature** | 20-25 degrees C | Below 30 degrees C | IoT temperature sensor |
| **Relative humidity** | 50-60% | Below 70% | IoT humidity sensor |
| **Moisture content** | 10.5-12% | Below 13% | Moisture meter (weekly check) |
| **Light exposure** | Minimal | No direct sunlight | Visual inspection |
| **Ventilation** | Moderate | No stagnant air | Visual/olfactory inspection |
| **Pest control** | Integrated pest management | No evidence of infestation | Regular inspection |

### Packaging

| Packaging | Cost (THB/unit) | Capacity | Shelf Life | Notes |
|-----------|----------------|----------|------------|-------|
| **GrainPro bag** | 150-350 | 30-70 kg | 12-18 months | Hermetic; best quality preservation |

### Community Processing Stations

Northern Thailand's coffee processing infrastructure has developed significantly since the Royal Project's initial investments in the 1970s-1980s. Today, a network of community processing stations, Royal Project facilities, and private specialty mills serves the region's estimated 30,000+ coffee-farming households.

| Facility Type | Number (est.) | Services | Fee Structure |
|---------------|--------------|----------|---------------|
| **Royal Project stations** | 15-20 | Full processing, quality control, marketing | Subsidized; 5-15 THB/kg cherry |
| **Community cooperative stations** | 30-50 | Depulping, fermentation, drying | Member rate: 10-20 THB/kg; non-member: 15-30 THB/kg |
| **Private specialty mills** | 10-20 | Full specialty processing, export preparation | 20-50 THB/kg cherry (varies by method) |
| **Individual farmer facilities** | 200+ | Basic depulping and drying | Self-managed; equipment investment |

### Royal Project Foundation Facilities

The Royal Project Foundation operates some of the most well-equipped processing stations in Northern Thailand, with facilities at key highland sites. These stations provide subsidized processing services to member farmers and serve as training centers for specialty processing methods. Key locations include stations at Doi Inthanon, Doi Pui, and Mae Chaem, each equipped with motorized depulpers, concrete fermentation tanks, raised bed drying facilities, and basic quality control equipment.

### Cooperative Models

The cooperative model is particularly well-suited to Northern Thailand's smallholder farming structure, where average farm sizes are 5-20 rai (0.8-3.2 hectares). Cooperatives pool resources to purchase processing equipment that individual farmers could not afford, and they aggregate enough volume to access specialty markets and export channels. Notable examples include the Doi Chang Coffee Cooperative (Akha community, Chiang Rai) and the Mae Hong Son Coffee Group.

## Northern Thailand Context

Northern Thailand possesses several natural and institutional advantages for coffee post-harvest processing that, when properly leveraged, position the region for continued growth in specialty coffee markets. The cool, dry highland climate during the November-February harvest season provides ideal conditions for both fermentation control and sun-drying — lower ambient temperatures slow fermentation to manageable rates, while the dry season ensures reliable sun-drying with minimal mold risk. This is a significant advantage over tropical lowland origins where high temperatures and humidity make controlled processing far more difficult.

The region's abundant highland water sources — springs, streams, and small reservoirs fed by monsoon rainfall — provide the water necessary for wet processing. Many highland communities have natural spring water piped to processing stations by gravity feed, eliminating pumping costs. However, water scarcity can develop in late dry season (March-April), so water-efficient processing methods and water recycling systems are increasingly important.

The community processing model, pioneered by the Royal Project Foundation and expanded through cooperative networks, gives smallholder farmers access to processing equipment and expertise that would be unaffordable individually. A farmer with 10 rai of coffee producing 1,500-2,500 kg of cherry per season cannot justify a 15,000 THB motorized depulper alone, but a cooperative serving 30-50 farmers can invest in depulpers, fermentation tanks, raised beds, and even innovative processing equipment while sharing costs across the group.

Specialty processing training programs have expanded significantly since 2016. The Royal Project Foundation, the Department of Agriculture, RMUTL, and private sector actors like Pacamara Coffee and various specialty roasters offer workshops and ongoing technical support in controlled fermentation methods, quality assessment, and market access. The Thailand Coffee Hub and annual Thai Coffee Festival provide additional networking and learning opportunities. The Cup of Excellence competition, held in Thailand for the first time in 2025, has further incentivized quality-focused processing by demonstrating that Northern Thai coffees can compete at the international level.

The EU-protected Geographical Indication (GI) status for Doi Tung coffee (2006 domestic, EU 2015) and Doi Chang coffee (EU 2015) provides a market advantage that rewards careful processing and quality consistency. Farmers within these GI zones have a strong incentive to maintain processing standards that protect the reputation and price premium of their regional brand.

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Start with washed process and master it before innovating.** The wet process provides the most consistent, predictable results and the clearest expression of terroir. Master quality control at each stage — float tank, depulping calibration, fermentation monitoring, drying — before attempting innovative methods.

2. **Invest in a float tank and pH meter as the first quality upgrades.** These two low-cost tools (500-3,000 THB for the tank; 500-3,000 THB for the pH meter) provide the biggest quality improvement per baht spent. Float tank separation removes 5-15% defective cherries; pH monitoring prevents over-fermentation.

3. **Monitor fermentation with IoT temperature sensors.** Deploy waterproof temperature sensors (200-500 THB per node) in fermentation tanks with mobile phone alerts. Northern Thailand's cool highland temperatures mean fermentation takes longer, and real-time monitoring prevents both under- and over-fermentation. See [[Temperature-Sensors]] for compatible hardware.

4. **Use raised beds for drying, not patios.** The additional airflow from raised beds produces more even drying and reduces mold risk. At 500-2,000 THB per bed, the investment is modest and the quality improvement is significant. Install rain covers for weather protection.

5. **Target the November-February dry season for harvest and processing.** The cool, dry weather provides ideal fermentation and drying conditions. Late-season coffee (March) faces higher rain risk and may require covered drying or mechanical backup.

6. **Measure moisture content before selling.** Use a pin-type moisture meter (2,000-8,000 THB) to verify that parchment or green bean reaches 10-12% moisture. Under-dried coffee develops mold; over-dried coffee is brittle and produces more defects during hulling.

7. **Store as parchment in GrainPro bags for maximum shelf life.** Parchment storage with hermetic GrainPro bags (150-350 THB per bag) preserves quality for 12-18 months, giving farmers flexibility to wait for favorable market prices rather than selling immediately at harvest.

8. **Join or form a processing cooperative.** Cooperative membership reduces per-farmer equipment costs, provides access to processing expertise, and creates sufficient volume to access specialty markets and export channels. Typical cooperative processing fees of 10-20 THB/kg cherry are far less than individual equipment investment.

9. **Experiment with innovative methods on small lots first.** Before committing an entire harvest to Semi-Carbonic Maceration or anaerobic fermentation, process 50-100 kg of cherry using the new method and evaluate the results. This limits financial risk while building experience.

10. **Maintain detailed processing records.** Record fermentation duration, temperature, pH readings, drying times, weather conditions, and cupping results for every lot. This data is invaluable for refining processing protocols, demonstrating traceability to buyers, and identifying problems before they become costly. IoT data logging systems make record-keeping nearly automatic.
