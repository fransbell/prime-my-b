# Grafted Seedlings & Rootstock Selection — Thailand

> **Summary**: How grafting Arabica scions onto Robusta rootstock can expand Arabica cultivation into lower, warmer elevations in Northern Thailand while maintaining quality — covering rootstock varieties, grafting techniques, success rates, cost comparisons, IoT monitoring differences, and practical recommendations for Thai farmers.

---

## Why Grafting Matters for Northern Thailand

Northern Thailand's coffee landscape is defined by a sharp altitude boundary: Arabica thrives above 800m, while below 600m only Robusta is viable. Between 600–800m lies a "decision boundary" zone (see [[Variety-Selection-Guide]]) where neither species performs optimally — Arabica suffers heat stress and increased coffee leaf rust (CLR) pressure, while Robusta faces cold nights that suppress growth. This marginal zone encompasses significant agricultural land in provinces like Lampang, Phayao, lower Chiang Mai (Mae Taeng, Chom Thong lowlands), and parts of Nan and Tak. Climate change is pushing this boundary upward (see [[Climate-Change-Impact]]), meaning areas that were once suitable for Arabica at 800m are becoming increasingly marginal.

Grafting Arabica scions onto Robusta rootstock offers a potential solution to this altitude squeeze. The Robusta root system provides heat tolerance, nematode resistance, partial CLR resistance through root-mediated stress buffering, and drought resilience through deeper root penetration. Meanwhile, the Arabica scion produces the quality beans that command premium prices in Thailand's growing specialty market (280–1,000+ THB/kg vs. 40–120 THB/kg for commodity). This combination could allow farmers at 600–800m to grow quality Arabica where it would otherwise fail, and could extend the viable range for existing Arabica farms facing warming temperatures.

However, grafted coffee is not a silver bullet. Cup quality may differ slightly from own-root Arabica, shade remains essential at lower elevations, and the graft union itself introduces a vulnerability point. Understanding the full picture — benefits, limitations, techniques, costs, and IoT monitoring implications — is essential before investing in grafted seedlings.

## Why Graft: The Rootstock Advantage

### Heat Tolerance

Robusta rootstock provides measurably improved heat tolerance for the grafted Arabica scion. The deeper, more extensive root system of Robusta (penetrating 1–2m+ vs. Arabica's typical 0.5–1m) accesses soil moisture at greater depth during hot periods, maintaining transpiration cooling when shallow-rooted Arabica would shut down. Research from Brazil and India shows grafted Arabica maintains photosynthetic rates 20–35% higher than own-root Arabica at temperatures above 28°C, precisely the range that becomes problematic at Northern Thailand's 600–800m zone during the hot season (March–May). The rootstock doesn't change the scion's intrinsic temperature preferences, but it prevents the catastrophic physiological collapse that occurs when shallow Arabica roots can't supply enough water to cool the canopy.

### Disease Resistance

| Disease | Robusta Rootstock Benefit | Mechanism | Limitation |
|---------|--------------------------|-----------|------------|
| **Nematodes (Meloidogyne spp.)** | **HIGH resistance** | Robusta roots are non-host or poor host for root-knot nematodes that devastate Arabica roots in warmer soils | Nematode species vary; some Robusta clones more resistant than others |
| **CLR (Hemileia vastatrix)** | **PARTIAL indirect benefit** | Healthier root system = more vigorous plant = better natural defense; Robusta's Shrz gene not expressed in Arabica scion leaves | Does NOT make the Arabica scion CLR-resistant; scion remains fully susceptible |
| **Root rot (Armillaria, Fusarium)** | **MODERATE benefit** | Robusta's more vigorous root system can compartmentalize decay better than Arabica | Does not eliminate risk, especially in waterlogged conditions |
| **Coffee Berry Disease** | No benefit | CBD attacks cherries, not roots | Not applicable |

The most significant disease benefit is nematode resistance. In Northern Thailand's warmer lowland soils (where soil temperatures can exceed 28°C at 20cm depth — see [[Temperature-Sensors]]), nematode pressure increases substantially. Grafted plants avoid the root galling, stunting, and yield decline that nematodes cause in own-root Arabica at these elevations.

### Drought Resistance

Robusta's root system architecture provides genuine drought resistance — but through a different mechanism than commonly understood. Robusta is NOT physiologically more drought-tolerant than Arabica (see [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]] for the full comparison). Rather, Robusta *avoids* drought stress by accessing deeper soil moisture through its extensive taproot and lateral root network. When grafted Arabica is planted on Robusta rootstock, it inherits this deeper water access, allowing the scion to maintain turgor pressure and photosynthetic function during dry periods that would wilt own-root Arabica. In Northern Thailand's hot-dry season (February–May), this difference can be the margin between a surviving plant and a dead one at 600–800m elevation.

The key comparison is between grafted Arabica at 600–800m and own-root Arabica at the same marginal elevation — not between grafted and ideal-elevation own-root. At 600–800m, grafted plants consistently outperform own-root Arabica because the Robusta rootstock prevents the root-system failures (nematodes, heat stress, drought) that plague shallow-rooted Arabica in warmer soils.

| Metric | Own-Root Arabica (1,000m+) | Grafted Arabica/Robusta (700m) | Own-Root Arabica (700m) |

| **Flavor clarity** | Excellent | Good | Muddled |

Cup quality research on grafted Arabica is limited but consistent: grafted plants typically score 1–3 SCA points below own-root Arabica at the same elevation, but 3–6 points above own-root Arabica at the same marginal (lower) elevation where the own-root plants are under heat and root stress. The slight quality reduction from grafting appears to come from subtle differences in nutrient uptake and water relations at the graft union, but the effect is small compared to the devastating quality impact of growing own-root Arabica at unsuitably low elevations.

## Rootstock Varieties Available in Thailand

### Chumphon Robusta Clones as Rootstock

The Chumphon Horticultural Research Center (see [[Robusta-for-North-Thailand]]) maintains the primary Robusta germplasm in Thailand. These clones, developed for Southern Thailand's conditions, have characteristics that make them suitable rootstock candidates for grafted Arabica in Northern Thailand.

| Rootstock Clone | Source | Root Vigor | Nematode Resistance | Drought Tolerance | Soil Adaptation | Availability |
|----------------|--------|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Chumphon 1** | DOA selection (1989+) | High | Good | Good | Wide | CHRC, DOA stations |
| **Chumphon 2** | Nestle/France import (1999) | High | Good | Moderate | Good | CHRC, limited |
| **FRT 141** | Nestle breeding | Very High | Very Good | Good | Wide | CHRC field trials |
| **FRT 07** | Nestle breeding | High | Good | Good | Good | CHRC field trials |
| **Local Thai Robusta** | Various | Variable | Variable | Variable | Variable | Private nurseries |

**Recommended rootstock for Northern Thailand**: Chumphon 1 is currently the best-documented and most accessible rootstock choice. Its vigorous root system and broad soil adaptation make it suitable for the diverse soil conditions found at 600–800m in Northern Thailand provinces. FRT 141 shows superior vigor in research trials but has limited distribution outside the Chumphon research center.

### Rootstock Selection Criteria

When selecting Robusta rootstock for grafting Arabica in Northern Thailand, consider these factors:

1. **Root vigor**: The primary purpose of the rootstock is to provide a more vigorous, deeper root system than own-root Arabica. Chumphon 1 and FRT-series clones all demonstrate strong taproot development.
2. **Graft compatibility**: Not all Robusta clones graft equally well with all Arabica varieties. Chumphon 1 shows good compatibility with Chiang Mai 80 and Typica in Thai trials. Compatibility with Geisha is less documented.
3. **Nematode resistance**: Critical at lower elevations where soil temperatures favor nematode reproduction. Chumphon 1 and FRT 141 have documented resistance.
4. **Sucker management**: Robusta rootstock will produce suckers that must be removed promptly. Some clones sucker more aggressively than others; Chumphon 1 is moderate in sucker production.
5. **Graft union stability**: The junction between scion and rootstock must remain strong for the plant's productive life. Incompatible combinations develop weak unions that break under wind or heavy fruit load.

## Grafting Techniques: Step-by-Step

Two grafting methods have proven effective for coffee in Thailand's climate: splice graft (also called whip graft) and side-veneer graft. Both are performed during the active growing season when cambium tissue is actively dividing, which in Northern Thailand means the early rainy season (May–July).

### Splice Graft (Whip Graft)

The splice graft is the simpler and faster technique, suitable for production-scale nursery operations. It works best when scion and rootstock diameters are closely matched (within 1–2mm).

**Timing**: May–July (early rainy season; high humidity promotes callus formation)

**Materials needed**:
- Sharp grafting knife or razor blade (sterilized with 70% alcohol)
- Grafting tape (parafilm or PVC grafting tape, 1.5cm width)
- Shade house (50–70% shade net)
- Spray bottle for misting
- Rootstock seedlings (6–8 months old, 4–5mm stem diameter at graft point)
- Scion wood (current-season growth, 3–4 nodes, from selected Arabica mother trees)

**Procedure**:

1. **Prepare rootstock** (Day −1): Select healthy Robusta seedlings with stem diameter 4–5mm at 10–15cm height. Water well the day before grafting. Remove any lateral shoots below graft point.
2. **Collect scion wood** (Day 0, early morning): Select semi-hardwood shoots from productive, disease-free Arabica mother trees (Chiang Mai 80, Typica, or Caturra). Each scion should have 3–4 nodes with the terminal bud intact. Cut scions 8–10cm long. Place in moist cloth immediately.
3. **Make rootstock cut**: Cut the rootstock stem horizontally at 10–15cm height. Then make a single sloping cut 2–3cm long downward through the stem, exposing a flat cambium surface.
4. **Make scion cut**: Make a matching sloping cut 2–3cm long at the base of the scion, creating a tongue of similar length and angle to the rootstock cut.
5. **Join scion and rootstock**: Align the cambium layers of scion and rootstock carefully. Even if diameters differ slightly, ensure at least one side has perfect cambium-to-cambium contact.
6. **Wrap the graft**: Wrap firmly with grafting tape from bottom to top, covering the entire cut surface. The tape should be tight enough to hold the join securely but not so tight as to crush the tissue.
7. **Place in recovery**: Move grafted plants to a shaded recovery area (70% shade). Maintain humidity at 80–90% by misting 3–4 times daily for the first 2 weeks.
8. **Monitor graft union**: After 14–21 days, successful grafts will show new growth from the scion bud. Unsuccessful grafts will show scion wilting and browning.
9. **Remove tape**: After 30–45 days, when the graft union has calloused and new growth is evident, carefully remove grafting tape to prevent girdling.
10. **Harden off**: Gradually reduce shade from 70% to 50% over 4–6 weeks. Reduce misting frequency. Plants are ready for field planting after 4–6 months of post-graft growth.

### Side-Veneer Graft

The side-veneer graft is preferred when scion and rootstock diameters differ, or when the nursery wants to keep the rootstock intact as a backup (the rootstock can re-sprout if the graft fails). It produces a stronger graft union but requires more skill.

**Timing**: June–August (slightly later than splice graft, when rootstock is more mature)

**Procedure**:

1. **Prepare rootstock**: Select Robusta seedlings 8–10 months old with stem diameter 5–7mm. Do NOT decapitate the rootstock — the graft is made on the side of the stem.
2. **Make rootstock flap**: At 10–15cm height, make a shallow downward cut 2.5–3cm long, slicing into the bark and cambium but NOT cutting through the stem. At the base of this cut, make a short upward cut to create a flap of bark and cambium tissue.
3. **Prepare scion**: Cut the scion (3–4 nodes, 8–10cm) with a long sloping cut on one side (2.5–3cm) and a short cut on the opposite side (0.5cm), creating a wedge shape.
4. **Insert scion**: Lift the bark flap on the rootstock and insert the scion wedge underneath, with the long cut surface facing the wood of the rootstock (cambium-to-cambium contact).
5. **Wrap and secure**: Wrap the graft union firmly with grafting tape. Then cut off the rootstock top above the graft point, redirecting all growth energy to the scion.
6. **Aftercare**: Same as splice graft — shaded recovery area, high humidity, gradual hardening off.

### Grafting Success Rates

| Technique | Expected Success Rate | Skill Level | Time per Graft | Best For |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|----------------|----------|
| **Splice graft** | 70–85% | Moderate | 2–3 minutes | Production nurseries, matched diameters |
| **Side-veneer graft** | 80–90% | High | 4–5 minutes | Variable diameters, stronger union needed |
| **With mist system** | 85–92% | Any | — | Professional nurseries with infrastructure |
| **Without mist system** | 60–75% | Any | — | Low-tech nursery, higher risk |

Key factors affecting success:
- **Cambium alignment**: The single most important factor. Even 1mm of misalignment dramatically reduces success.
- **Humidity during healing**: 80–90% RH for the first 2 weeks is critical. Grafted plants in Northern Thailand's hot season (March–May) fail at high rates without mist systems.
- **Scion freshness**: Scions must be grafted within 4–6 hours of collection. Wilted scions have very low success rates.
- **Knife sharpness**: A dull blade crushes cambium cells instead of cutting cleanly, reducing callus formation.
- **Rootstock health**: Nematode-infected or nutrient-deficient rootstock produces poor graft unions.

### Private Nurseries (Limited Availability)

Currently, no private nursery in Thailand specializes in grafted coffee seedlings as a commercial product. Farmers interested in grafted plants must:
1. Request custom grafting from established nurseries (e.g., CoffeeDeep — see [[Seed-Source-Thailand]])
2. Learn grafting techniques through DOA or Royal Project training programs
3. Establish a small grafting operation on their own farm

**Maejo University** in Chiang Mai has conducted the most extensive grafting research in Thailand and periodically offers training workshops. Contact the Faculty of Agricultural Production for scheduling.

### Future Availability

The Royal Project Foundation's grafted Arabica trials (see Case Studies below) may lead to commercial-scale grafted seedling production if results continue to be positive. Farmers should monitor Royal Project announcements through their website (royalproject.org) and the HRDI knowledge base (hkm.hrdi.or.th).

## IoT Monitoring Differences for Grafted Plants

Grafted plants with Robusta rootstock behave differently from own-root Arabica in ways that directly affect IoT sensor thresholds and monitoring strategies. The decision system (see [[Decision-Logic]]) and threshold tables (see [[Sensor-Metrics-Thresholds]]) must be parameterized for grafted plants.

### Soil Moisture Thresholds

Robusta roots penetrate deeper (1–2m+) than Arabica roots (0.5–1m), meaning grafted plants access moisture from a larger soil volume. This has important implications for soil moisture sensor readings taken at the standard 15–30cm depth:

| Parameter | Own-Root Arabica | Grafted Arabica/Robusta | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|-----------|
| **Optimal VWC (fruit development)** | 30–40% | 25–35% | Deeper roots access sub-surface moisture; surface readings less critical |
| **Warning VWC** | 25–30% | 20–25% | Grafted plants can tolerate lower surface moisture due to deep root access |
| **Critical VWC** | <25% | <18% | Only critical when deep soil moisture is also depleted |
| **Irrigation trigger** | <25% VWC | <20% VWC (surface) BUT verify at 40–60cm depth | Surface reading alone is misleading |
| **Post-irrigation target** | 30–35% | 25–30% (surface) | Grafted plants need less surface watering; deep infiltration is more important |

**Critical IoT Implementation Note**: For grafted plants, a single soil moisture sensor at 15cm depth provides an incomplete picture. The recommended deployment is dual-depth sensors: one at 15cm (standard) and one at 40–60cm (deep). If the deep sensor shows adequate moisture (>20% VWC), surface dryness is less concerning. If both depths are dry, the irrigation urgency matches own-root Arabica. This dual-depth approach should be configured in the [[Decision-Logic]] engine with a `grafted_plant: true` flag.

### Soil Temperature Thresholds

Robusta rootstock is more tolerant of warm soil temperatures than Arabica roots:

| Parameter | Own-Root Arabica | Grafted Arabica/Robusta |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Optimal soil temp** | 18–22°C | 20–24°C (rootstock prefers warmer) |
| **Warning soil temp** | 22–28°C | 24–30°C |
| **Critical soil temp** | >28°C | >32°C |

At 600–800m elevation in Northern Thailand, soil temperatures at 20cm depth routinely reach 24–28°C during the hot season. This is within the warning zone for own-root Arabica but acceptable for grafted plants, reducing the number of heat-stress alerts generated.

### Alert Priority Adjustments

When the decision system identifies a `grafted` plant type, several alert thresholds should be adjusted:

| Alert Type | Own-Root Arabica Priority | Grafted Arabica/Robusta Priority | Change Rationale |
|------------|--------------------------|--------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Surface soil moisture low** | WARNING/CRITICAL | INFO/WARNING (check deep sensor first) | Deep roots compensate |
| **Soil temperature high** | WARNING | INFO (if <32°C) | Robusta roots tolerate warmth |
| **Nematode risk (soil temp >25°C)** | CRITICAL | INFO (resistant rootstock) | Rootstock is nematode-resistant |
| **CLR risk (humidity + leaf wetness)** | Same as own-root | Same as own-root | Scion is NOT CLR-resistant |
| **Drought stress** | CRITICAL | WARNING (deeper roots buy time) | More time to respond but still needs action |
| **Sucker emergence** | N/A | WARNING (monthly check) | Robusta rootstock produces suckers that compete with scion |

## Limitations: Grafting is Not a Silver Bullet

### Cup Quality Differences

Grafted Arabica consistently scores 1–3 SCA points below own-root Arabica grown at the same elevation. This quality gap is attributed to:
- **Nutrient translocation inefficiency**: The graft union creates a slight bottleneck for water and nutrient transport, particularly under heavy fruit load. This can result in slightly less complex sugar profiles in the cherry.
- **Rootstock influence on flavor**: Robusta root physiology processes soil nutrients differently than Arabica roots. Subtle differences in mineral uptake (particularly potassium and magnesium) can affect cup body and acidity.
- **Processing compensation**: The quality gap can be partially closed through innovative processing methods (SCM, yeast process — see [[Arabica-for-North-Thailand]]). Well-processed grafted Arabica at 700m can achieve SCA 80+ (specialty grade), though reaching 84+ is more challenging than with own-root plants at 1,000m+.

### Shade Remains Essential

Grafted plants at 600–800m still require 40–60% shade for quality production. The Robusta rootstock does NOT make the Arabica scion more tolerant of full sun exposure. Without shade, grafted plants will produce higher yields of lower-quality beans, and the scion may suffer sunburn damage. Shade tree species recommended for Northern Thailand include Leucaena leucocephala, Gliricidia sepium, and Erythrina species (see [[Coffee-Sunlight-Requirements]]).

### Graft Union Vulnerability

The graft union point (typically 10–15cm above ground) is structurally weaker than a natural stem. Risks include:
- **Wind breakage**: Grafted plants on steep Northern Thailand slopes are vulnerable to wind damage at the graft union, especially during monsoon storms (May–October).
- **Disease entry**: Improperly healed graft unions can serve as entry points for fungal pathogens (Fusarium, Phytophthora). Proper grafting technique and complete callus formation before field planting are essential.
- **Sucker competition**: Robusta rootstock produces vigorous suckers that, if not removed, will outcompete the Arabica scion for resources. Monthly sucker inspection is mandatory.

### CLR Susceptibility Unchanged

The most critical limitation: **grafting onto Robusta rootstock does NOT make the Arabica scion resistant to coffee leaf rust.** The scion leaves remain fully susceptible. Farmers at 600–800m with grafted plants still need CLR management strategies (copper-based fungicides, pruning for airflow, resistant variety selection — see [[Pest-Disease-Management]]). The rootstock's only CLR benefit is indirect: a healthier root system produces a more vigorous plant that can better tolerate some defoliation.

## Practical Recommendations for Northern Thailand Farmers

1. **Consider grafting if your farm is at 600–800m elevation** with north-facing slopes and existing shade trees. Grafting offers the most value precisely where own-root Arabica is marginal — not at high elevations where Arabica already thrives, and not at low elevations where even grafted Arabica cannot produce quality coffee.

2. **Use Chiang Mai 80 as the scion variety** for grafted plants at 600–800m. Its CLR resistance (from the HDT/Robusta introgression) is critical at lower elevations where CLR pressure is intense. Typica and Geisha scions on Robusta rootstock remain CLR-susceptible and are not recommended below 800m.

3. **Source Chumphon 1 rootstock seedlings from the Chumphon Horticultural Research Center** (see [[Robusta-for-North-Thailand]]) and arrange grafting through DOA or Maejo University training programs. Currently, custom grafting is the only option; there is no commercial grafted seedling market.

4. **Invest in dual-depth soil moisture sensors** if using grafted plants. Standard single-depth (15cm) sensors will trigger unnecessary irrigation alerts because the Robusta rootstock accesses deeper moisture. Configure the [[Decision-Logic]] system with the `grafted_plant` flag to apply adjusted thresholds.

5. **Plant shade trees 12 months before grafted seedlings.** At 600–800m, shade is even more critical for grafted plants than for highland Arabica because ambient temperatures are higher. Leucaena and Gliricidia are fast-growing options that provide shade within 12–18 months.

6. **Budget 40,000–82,000 THB/rai for establishment** (first 3 years) with grafted plants, compared to 27,000–56,000 THB for own-root Arabica at ideal elevation. The higher cost must be justified by the location advantage — if your land is only at 650m, the comparison is not "grafted vs. own-root at 1,000m" but "grafted at 650m vs. nothing at 650m" (or Robusta at 650m).

7. **Monthly sucker inspection is mandatory.** Robusta rootstock will produce vigorous suckers from below the graft union. These grow faster than the Arabica scion and will dominate the plant within 2–3 months if not removed. Train farm workers to identify and remove suckers promptly.

8. **Do not expect specialty-grade quality (>84 SCA) from grafted plants at 600–700m.** A realistic target is 78–82 SCA — high commodity to low specialty range. Focus marketing on the origin story, sustainable farming practices, and processing innovation (SCM, honey process) to add value beyond the raw cup score.

9. **Combine grafting with IoT monitoring for maximum benefit.** The real advantage of grafted plants in a sensor-monitored system is the ability to fine-tune irrigation to the deeper root zone, reduce unnecessary alerts from surface soil moisture readings, and optimize shade management with variety-specific thresholds (see [[Variety-Sensor-Profiles]]).

10. **Start small — trial 50–100 grafted plants before committing to a full plantation.** Grafted coffee in Northern Thailand is still experimental outside research stations. A small trial allows you to assess survival, growth rate, and cup quality under your specific microclimate before making a major investment.
