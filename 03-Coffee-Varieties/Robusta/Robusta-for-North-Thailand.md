# Robusta Varieties for North Thailand

> **Summary**: Guide to Robusta (Coffea canephora) varieties relevant to Thailand, covering the Chumphon Horticultural Research Center breeding program, FRT-series clones, GI-registered Robusta, and the emerging Northern Thailand Robusta opportunity at 200-800m elevation.

---

## Overview

Thailand is historically a Robusta-dominant producer, with Robusta accounting for approximately 80-90% of total coffee output by volume at peak production. While Northern Thailand is traditionally Arabica territory (800-1,200+ masl), Robusta has significant potential at lower elevations (200-800m) in several Northern and lower-Northern provinces. A "Robusta Renaissance" is underway in Thailand, driven by GI registration (Ranong Robusta saw a 7.5x price increase from 80 to 600 THB/kg), the Fine Robusta Academy of Thailand (world's first), and the CQI Q Robusta grading system. Understanding the available Robusta varieties and their adaptation to Northern Thailand conditions is essential for farmers in the 200-800m elevation zone.

**Key Facts:**
- Total Robusta cultivation area: approximately 75,396 rai productive (2022, declining from peak)
- Historical peak production: approximately 80,000 tonnes annually from Southern Thailand
- Recent production: approximately 13,000-19,000 tonnes (declining due to crop switching)
- Domestic consumption: approximately 75% of Robusta stays in Thailand (soluble, roasted, powdered, tinned coffee)
- Thai coffee market value: 65 billion THB in 2025 (8.33% YoY growth)

## Chumphon Horticultural Research Center — Thailand's Robusta Breeding Hub

### Institution Profile

The Chumphon Horticultural Research Center (CHRC) is the primary national center for Robusta coffee research, breeding, and variety development in Thailand. Operated by the Department of Agriculture (DOA), it has been active for 30+ years in Robusta coffee variety research.

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Location** | Chumphon Province, Southern Thailand |
| **Operated by** | Department of Agriculture (DOA), Ministry of Agriculture |
| **Staff** | Approximately 200 employees |
| **Area** | 280 hectares |
| **Primary mandates** | Coconut research + Robusta coffee research |

### Breeding Activities

- **More than 30 Robusta coffee varieties** are being processed and evaluated at CHRC
- The center conducts natural processing trials on these varieties
- Goal: Create new Robusta hybrids with richer flavors suitable for specialty markets
- CHRC distributes young shoots (clonal propagation material) to farmers
- The center collaborates with Nestle Agricultural Services (FRT varieties tested at Chumphon)
- CHRC is the primary source of DOA-recommended Robusta planting material for Thai farmers

### DOA-Registered Robusta Varieties

| Variety | Origin | Registration | Key Traits |
|---------|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Chumphon 1** | Selected from local germplasm since 1989; including line 1/11 from Ranat, Sawi District | DOA registered | Yields more than 2 kg seed/tree/year |
| **Chumphon 2** | Imported from France/Togo via Nestle (Thailand) in 1999; Congolese group | DOA registered | Tested at Chumphon center |

## FRT Series — The Most Significant Thai Robusta Clones

### Overview

The FRT (France Robusta Thailand/Nestle) series represents the most significant introduced Robusta germplasm in Thailand. These clones were developed by Nestle's R&D facility in France and field-tested in multiple countries including Thailand and the Philippines.

| Clone ID | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| **FRT 07** | Field-tested in Thailand and Philippines |
| **FRT 17** | Field-tested in Thailand and Philippines |
| **FRT 23** | Field-tested in Thailand and Philippines |
| **FRT 65** | Field-tested in Thailand and Philippines |
| **FRT 141** | Confirmed in Thai research trials at Chumphon; used in shade-grown Robusta studies |

### FRT 141 — Most Documented Thai FRT Clone

FRT 141 is the best-documented FRT clone in Thai academic research. It was featured in a ThaiJO study on Robusta cultivar plasticity under different light conditions, confirming that Nestle-developed FRT clones are actively used in Thai breeding programs and field trials. The study evaluated cultivar-specific responses to shade-grown versus full-sun conditions, which is highly relevant for Northern Thailand's agroforestry systems.

### Nestle's Broader Robusta Research

Recent Nestle research (May 2026) shows that planting a mix of 6 Robusta varieties can increase yields by up to 86% while improving cup quality. This mixed-variety approach is highly applicable to Thai Robusta farming and suggests farmers should plant multiple clones rather than a single variety. Nestle has also developed low-carbon Robusta varieties with up to 50% higher yields (announced 2024), though these may not yet be available in Thailand.

## Conilon (Kouillou) Variety — Future Potential

### Overview

Conilon and "Robusta" are different genetic groups within the same species (Coffea canephora), similar to how Arabica has different cultivars. Conilon originates from the Kouilou River area in southern Gabon and is primarily grown in Brazil (Espirito Santo state).

### Why Conilon Matters for Thailand

| Factor | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Specialty potential** | Brazilian BRS Conilon varieties score 80-87 points on SCA scale |
| **Cup characteristics** | Smoother body, chocolatey depth, lower bitterness than standard Robusta |
| **Growth cycle** | Shorter than typical Robusta |
| **Climate adaptation** | Thrives in warm, humid conditions similar to Southern Thailand |
| **Current Thai presence** | Not documented as a major planting; some Thai Robusta may contain Conilon-type genetics |

Conilon germplasm could be valuable for Thai Robusta improvement through cross-breeding with existing CHRC clones to improve cup quality while maintaining adaptation to Thai conditions.

## Thai GI-Registered Robusta Coffees

As of 2025, three of Thailand's top 5 GI-registered coffees are Robusta — a testament to the quality transformation underway:

| GI Name | Type | Province | Price (THB/kg) | Price Increase | Flavor Profile |
|---------|------|----------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Ranong Coffee** | Robusta | Ranong | 600 (from 80) | **7.5x** | Bold, deep Robusta; 100-year heritage |
| **Khao Thalu (Chumphon)** | Robusta | Chumphon | 450 (from 230) | 1.95x | Strong, full-bodied; grown with natural bat guano |
| **Tham Sing (Chumphon)** | Robusta | Chumphon | 850 (from 500) | 1.7x | Rich, smooth; fruity aromas likened to cherry |

**Key Insight:** Ranong Robusta's 7.5x price increase (80 to 600 THB/kg) is the most dramatic value transformation in Thai coffee. GI registration has been the single most effective price driver for Robusta, and this model is replicable for Northern Thailand Robusta producers.

## Northern Thailand Robusta — The Emerging Opportunity

### Confirmed Robusta Plantings in the North

#### Nan Province
- Green bean traders list "Thailand Nan Robusta G1 Natural" at 800-1,150 masl
- Natural/dry process; tasting notes: dark chocolate, earthy, full-bodied
- Demonstrates that Robusta can be grown at relatively high altitudes in Northern Thailand, potentially yielding better cup quality

#### Tak Province (Mo Kro / Umphang)
- Tambon Mo Kro, Umphang District; interspersed with banana, avocado, and macadamia trees
- Shade-grown/agroforestry; gaining attention as "the best Robusta coffee in Thailand"
- Both large and small growers operate in the area

#### Uttaradit Province
- Border province between North and Central Thailand
- Has areas suitable for Robusta (below 800m); limited but existing cultivation

### Climate Match for Northern Thailand Robusta

| Parameter | Southern Thailand (Traditional) | Northern Thailand (Emerging) |
|-----------|--------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Altitude range** | 0-300m (mostly lowland) | 200-1,150m (foothills/valleys) |
| **Annual rainfall** | 2,000-3,000mm | 1,200-1,600mm (may need irrigation) |
| **Temperature range** | 26-34 degrees C (hot, humid year-round) | 20-33 degrees C (cooler dry season) |
| **Humidity** | 75-90% year-round | 60-85% (seasonal) |
| **Disease pressure** | Higher (warm + humid) | Lower (cooler, drier) |
| **Cup quality potential** | Good (GI-recognized) | Potentially higher (higher altitude, slower maturation) |
| **Processing** | Natural/dry dominant | Natural/dry; experimental honey/washed |

**Critical Consideration for Northern Thailand:** Rainfall in Northern Thailand (1,200-1,600mm) is significantly lower than Southern Thailand (2,000-3,000mm). Irrigation may be essential during the dry season (February-April) for Robusta in Northern Thailand. However, the cooler temperatures and lower disease pressure at higher Northern altitudes may produce superior cup quality.

## Climate Requirements for Robusta

| Parameter | Optimal Range | Thailand Context |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Temperature (mean)** | 22-28 degrees C | Southern Thailand: well within range; Northern lowlands: suitable |
| **Altitude** | 0-800m (traditional); up to 1,200m possible | Southern: 0-300m; Northern: 200-1,150m (confirmed) |
| **Annual rainfall** | 1,500-2,500mm | Southern: 2,000-3,000mm (adequate); Northern: 1,200-1,600mm (may need irrigation) |
| **Dry season** | 2-3 months (for flowering trigger) | Southern: short dry season; Northern: more pronounced dry season |
| **Humidity** | 70-90% | Southern: consistently high; Northern: seasonal |
| **Soil pH** | 5.5-6.5 | Generally suitable in Thailand's coffee areas |
| **Shade** | 30-50% shade recommended | Agroforestry systems common in both regions |

### Climate Change Considerations

- Thailand ranks third globally for increase in 30+ degrees C days, which threatens coffee cultivation
- Robusta is more heat-tolerant than Arabica, making it a potential climate adaptation strategy for lower elevations
- However, recent research (Kath et al., 2020) shows Robusta's optimal temperature is lower than previously assumed (less than or equal to 20.5 degrees C mean), suggesting even Robusta may face climate stress
- The GIZ Coffee++ project is actively training Thai Robusta farmers on climate change adaptation and regenerative agriculture practices

## Processing Methods for Thai Robusta

| Method | Prevalence | Result | Notes |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Natural/Dry** | More than 90% | Heavy body, low acidity, chocolatey/earthy | Southern Thailand's dry season coincides with harvest; ideal for sun-drying |
| **Washed/Wet** | Very limited but growing | Cleaner cup, more acidity, lighter body | Water availability and infrastructure are barriers; growing with specialty movement |
| **Honey/Pulped Natural** | Very rare; experimental | Sweet, fruity, complex | Good option for specialty differentiation |

**Traditional Thai Robusta Use:** Dark roasted for traditional Thai coffee (oliang). Specialty emergence: medium roasts highlighting chocolate, nut, and spice notes.

## Specialty Robusta Movement in Thailand

### Fine Robusta Academy of Thailand

- **Location**: Chumphon Province
- **Purpose**: Training Robusta producers in cupping, quality evaluation, and specialty processing
- **Significance**: First dedicated Fine Robusta training center globally
- **Programs**: CQI Intro Cupping classes, Q Robusta Grader certification courses

### Q Project Thailand — Robusta Cohort

- Launched 2024 with separate Arabica and Robusta tracks
- Robusta cohort focuses on Chumphon and Southern Thailand producers
- Thai Robusta coffees featured at SCA Expo Chicago, World of Coffee Busan, and World of Coffee Copenhagen
- 2024 directory featured 32 Arabica and 3 Robusta coffees — Robusta is underrepresented but growing

## Northern Thailand Context

Northern Thailand's Robusta opportunity is distinct from the Southern Thailand tradition. At 200-800m elevation, Northern provinces like Nan, Tak (Umphang), and Uttaradit offer cooler temperatures, lower disease pressure, and the potential for higher cup quality due to slower cherry maturation at altitude. The main challenges are lower rainfall (requiring irrigation investment), lack of established processing infrastructure, and absence of GI registration for Northern Robusta. However, the success of Southern GI Robustas (7.5x price increase for Ranong) provides a proven roadmap: invest in quality processing, pursue GI registration, and connect with the specialty Robusta market through Q Project Thailand and the Fine Robusta Academy.

The GIZ Coffee++ project (Thai-German cooperation) is actively training Robusta farmers on regenerative agriculture and climate adaptation, targeting 2,200 Robusta farmers across 4 southern provinces. Extending this support to Northern Thailand's emerging Robusta areas would accelerate development.

---

## Practical Recommendations

1. **For Northern Thailand farmers (200-800m):** Source Chumphon 1 or FRT-series clones from CHRC. Plant multiple clones (minimum 3-4) for genetic diversity and yield optimization, per Nestle research showing 86% yield increase with mixed varieties.
2. **Pursue GI registration early** — this has been the single most effective price driver for Thai Robusta (7.5x for Ranong, 1.95x for Khao Thalu).
3. **Invest in specialty processing** (washed, honey) to differentiate from commodity Robusta and access the growing specialty Robusta market.
4. **Plan for irrigation** — Northern Thailand's 1,200-1,600mm rainfall is below Robusta's ideal; dry season (Feb-Apr) irrigation is essential.
5. **Connect with Q Project Thailand** for quality training, market access, and international exposure.
6. **Consider Conilon-type germplasm** for future specialty cup quality potential; work with CHRC on introduction and evaluation.
