# Arabica Varieties for Northern Thailand

> **Summary**: Comprehensive guide to Arabica coffee varieties cultivated in Northern Thailand, covering the dominant Chiang Mai 80 (Catimor), heritage Typica and Caturra, rising-star Geisha, and the foundational MLDT/HDT breeding lines that shaped Thai Arabica.

---

## Overview

Northern Thailand's Arabica coffee industry was born from the Royal Project initiative launched by King Bhumibol Adulyadej in the 1970s as an opium replacement crop for highland hill tribe communities (Akha, Lisu, Hmong, Karen, Lahu, Yao). The region spans four key provinces, each offering distinct microclimates and altitude zones that determine which Arabica varieties will thrive. Today, an estimated 70-80% of all Northern Thai Arabica is a single variety — Chiang Mai 80 (a Catimor-type selection) — but a growing specialty movement is reviving heritage varieties like Typica and introducing competition-grade Geisha. Understanding the strengths, weaknesses, and altitude requirements of each variety is essential for farmers seeking to maximize both quality and profitability in this unique terroir.

| Province | Key Growing Areas | Altitude Range | Notable Varieties |
|----------|------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Chiang Mai** | Doi Inthanon, Doi Pui, Doi Suthep, Mae Chaem, Chom Thong | 800-1,700 masl | Chiang Mai 80, Catimor, Geisha, Typica |
| **Chiang Rai** | Doi Chang, Doi Tung, Doi Wawi, Doi Pang Khon, Mae Chan | 800-1,500 masl | Catimor, Caturra, Catuai, Geisha |
| **Mae Hong Son** | Mae Sariang, Pang Mapha, Sop Moei | 800-1,400 masl | Typica, Caturra, Catuai, Bourbon, Geisha |
| **Nan** | Thung Chang, Tha Wang Pha, Baan Maneepruek | 1,000-1,600 masl | Geisha, Catimor, Typica |

**Key Statistics:**
- Total Arabica production: approximately 10,000 tons/year (vs. approximately 30,000 tons Robusta in the south)
- National yield average: 980 kg/hectare (FAO 2013); historical peak 198 kg/rai (1997/98)
- Planting density: approximately 177 trees/rai (3x3m spacing); 6.25 rai = 1 hectare
- Arabica elevation range: 800-1,700 masl
- Temperature range: 15-25 degrees C
- Rainfall requirement: 1,200-2,500 mm/year
- EU PGI status: Doi Tung coffee (2006, EU 2015), Doi Chang coffee (EU 2015)
- Thailand's average coffee farmer age: 25-35 years (youngest globally)

## Chiang Mai 80 (Catimor-Type) — The Dominant Variety

### Origin and Genetic Background

Chiang Mai 80 is the single most dominant Arabica variety in Northern Thailand, accounting for an estimated 70-80% of all Arabica plantings. It is not simply a standard Catimor but a specific Thai selection: the CIFC 7963 line was backcrossed with SL28 specifically to improve cup quality while retaining rust resistance. This gives Chiang Mai 80 the disease resistance of a Catimor combined with the flavor sophistication of a Kenyan selection. It was officially released in 2007 by the Department of Agriculture after three decades of selection under the Royal Project Foundation.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Parentage** | Caturra x Hibrido de Timor (HDT), backcrossed with SL28 |
| **Genetic Type** | Introgressed hybrid (contains Robusta genes via HDT) |
| **CIFC Line** | 7963 (F2 generation from 26 HDT derivative lines) |
| **Thai Selection** | Department of Agriculture, under Royal Project Foundation |
| **Year Released** | 2007 |
| **Named After** | Chiang Mai province + CIFC line 7963 |

### Altitude Range

| Zone | Altitude | Performance |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **Marginal** | 700-900 masl | Survives but lower quality; flatter cup |
| **Suitable** | 900-1,200 masl | Standard production; chocolate-dominant |
| **Optimal** | 1,000-1,400 masl | Best balance of yield and quality |
| **High** | 1,400-1,600 masl | Improved acidity and complexity |

### Cup Quality Potential

| Metric | Score/Description |
|--------|-------------------|
| **DOA Breeding Score** | 6.5-7.0/10 (vs. 5.5 for Caturra under same conditions) |
| **SCA Specialty Grade** | Well-grown and processed: 80-86 points |
| **Cup of Excellence** | Catimor lots have scored 86.73+ (COE 2025, Karen Farm Chomthong) |

**Typical Cup Profile:**
- Chocolate and cocoa — the most consistent descriptor; milk chocolate to dark bittersweet
- Caramel and brown sugar — gentle, rounded sweetness; low-to-moderate acidity
- Stone fruit and citrus — subtle peach, apricot, gentle lemon in lighter roasts
- Full body, low-to-moderate acidity — smooth, rounded mouthfeel
- Clean finish — the SL28 influence keeps the cup free of astringency

**Processing Impact:** The 2016 processing revolution in Thailand challenged the assumption that Catimor could not reach specialty grade. Semi-Carbonic Maceration (SCM), Yeast Process, Coffivino Process, and Brixter Process (developed by Rajamangala University of Technology Lanna) have all enhanced Catimor's expression, with competition-winning results.

### Disease Resistance

| Disease | Resistance Level | Notes |
|---------|-----------------|-------|
| **Coffee Leaf Rust (Race II)** | **HIGH** | Primary reason for adoption; inherited from HDT/Robusta genes |
| **New CLR races** | Moderate concern | New rust races are evolving; resistance may degrade over time |
| **Coffee Berry Borer (CBB)** | Susceptible | Increasing problem with warmer temperatures |
| **Drought** | **SUSCEPTIBLE** | Significant weakness; requires shade/intercropping |

### Yield Characteristics

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| **Typical yield (Thailand)** | 150-250 kg/rai (938-1,563 kg/ha) |
| **Well-managed yield** | Up to 300 kg/rai (1,875 kg/ha) |
| **Planting density** | 177 trees/rai (3x3m) to 250 trees/rai |
| **Time to first harvest** | 3 years |
| **Productive lifespan** | 15-20 years (shorter than Typica/SL28) |
| **Peak production** | Years 5-12 |

### Current Adoption in Northern Thailand

| Province | Districts/Areas | Estimated Share |
|----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Chiang Mai** | Mae Chaem, Chom Thong, Doi Pui, Doi Inthanon slopes | approximately 75% of Arabica area |
| **Chiang Rai** | Doi Chang, Doi Tung, Mae Chan, Wawi, Pang Khon | approximately 80% of Arabica area |
| **Mae Hong Son** | Mae Sariang, Sop Moei, Pang Mapha | approximately 60% of Arabica area |
| **Nan** | Tha Wang Pha, Thung Chang | approximately 65% of Arabica area |

## Typica — The Heritage Variety

### Origin and Genetic Background

Typica is one of the two foundational Arabica varieties (alongside Bourbon) that spread globally from Yemen. It was among the first varieties introduced to Northern Thailand through the Royal Project in the 1970s. While its susceptibility to coffee leaf rust caused it to lose dominance, cherished heirloom Typica trees from the early Royal Project period can still be found today, particularly in older, shade-grown gardens. Some 50+ year old Typica trees survive in shade-grown systems integrated with miang tea.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Yemen (via Java, Indonesia, then Amsterdam botanical garden, then the Americas) |
| **Genetic type** | Pure Arabica (no introgression) |
| **Related varieties** | Maragogipe, Kona, Blue Mountain, Java |
| **Introduction to Thailand** | 1970s, via Royal Project and UN pilot projects |

### Key Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Optimal altitude** | 1,200-1,600 masl |
| **SCA score range** | 82-88 points (well-grown at high altitude) |
| **Yield** | 80-150 kg/rai (500-938 kg/ha) |
| **CLR resistance** | **HIGHLY SUSCEPTIBLE** — primary reason for decline |
| **Lifespan** | 30+ years (very long-lived) |
| **Bean size** | Average to Large (screen 16-17) |
| **Current share** | 3-5% of Northern Thai Arabica — rare but prized |

**Typical Cup Profile:** Clean, sweet, delicate; floral and tea-like qualities at high altitude; bright, refined acidity; medium body with complex sweetness. Often described as "elegant" — the benchmark for classic Arabica character.

### Thailand-Specific Notes

- Typica was one of the first varieties planted under the Royal Project in the 1970s
- Largely replaced by Catimor due to CLR susceptibility after rust arrived in 1975
- Currently being rediscovered by specialty-focused farmers willing to manage rust risk
- Heirloom Typica and Bourbon varieties from early Royal Project period are "often prized for their cup quality and elegant characteristics" (Old Quarter Coffee)

## Caturra — The Compact Parent

### Origin and Genetic Background

Caturra was introduced to Northern Thailand through the Royal Project and UN pilot programs in the 1970s. It was initially popular for its compact growth and high yield, but proved devastatingly susceptible to coffee leaf rust when CLR arrived in 1975. Its compact stature and productivity made it a key genetic contributor to the Catimor hybrid, and its dwarfism trait was inherited by Chiang Mai 80.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Brazil (natural mutation of Bourbon, discovered early 1900s) |
| **Genetic type** | Natural mutation of Bourbon (pure Arabica, no introgression) |
| **Introduction to Thailand** | 1970s, via Royal Project and UN programs |

### Key Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Optimal altitude** | 1,200-1,500 masl |
| **SCA score range** | 80-86 points (well-grown) |
| **DOA breeding comparison** | 5.5/10 (vs. 6.5-7.0 for Chiang Mai 80 in same conditions) |
| **Yield** | 120-200 kg/rai (750-1,250 kg/ha) |
| **CLR resistance** | **VERY HIGHLY SUSCEPTIBLE** |
| **Lifespan** | 15-25 years |
| **Growth habit** | Compact/dwarf — suits dense planting and steep terrain |
| **Current share** | 3-5% of Northern Thai Arabica |

### Thailand-Specific Notes

- Doi Tung coffee is a blend of Catimor, Caturra, and Catuai, grown at 800-1,200 masl
- The compact growth habit of Caturra was inherited by Catimor/Chiang Mai 80, making it suitable for Northern Thailand's steep terrain
- Some Caturra still present in mixed-variety gardens, particularly in Mae Hong Son

## MLDT / HDT Selections — Thailand's Breeding Foundation

### Overview

The term "MLDT" refers to local Thai cultivar selections derived from Hibrido de Timor (HDT) lines received from CIFC Portugal. These are the foundational breeding material from which Chiang Mai 80 and other Thai Arabica varieties were developed. The DOA received F2 generation seeds from 26 lines of HDT derivatives in 1974, specifically targeted at addressing the CLR crisis that had devastated Caturra plantings.

### Key HDT Lines in Thailand

| Line | Notes |
|------|-------|
| **CIFC 7963** | Selected as Chiang Mai 80 (released 2007) |
| **H 528/21** | Bean size comparable to Caturra; good production potential |
| **H 528/46** | Coffee leaf rust resistance confirmed |
| **Other lines** | From the 26 received; various field selections ongoing |

### Genetic Diversity Research

A 2023 study using genotyping-by-sequencing analyzed 23 coffee genotypes planted at the Royal Project Foundation across different regions of Northern Thailand. The research confirmed significant genetic diversity among the HDT-derived lines and highlighted the importance of maintaining this germplasm for future breeding. The Royal Project Foundation maintains experimental plots with multiple HDT-derived lines and over 40 coffee varieties at stations in Chiang Mai.

### Thailand-Specific Notes

- Thailand's Arabica breeding program is one of the few in Southeast Asia to have produced a named, commercially released variety (Chiang Mai 80)
- The 1974 seed import from CIFC was specifically targeted at addressing the CLR crisis
- The genetic diversity within the 26 CIFC lines represents valuable breeding material for future climate adaptation
- No new named releases beyond Chiang Mai 80 and DOA Chiang Mai 1 as of 2026

## Geisha / Gesha — The Rising Star

### Origin and Genetic Background

Geisha is the most celebrated specialty coffee variety globally, and has become a rising star in Northern Thailand over the past decade. Thai Geisha has won national competitions and achieved auction prices up to 10,000 THB per kilogram (Baan Maneepruek, Nan, 2023). The variety was pioneered in Nan province by Kaleb Jordan, who obtained seeds from Hacienda La Esmeralda in Panama around 2005. After approximately 20 years of cultivation, his Baan Maneepruek Geisha won Thailand's Best Specialty Coffee Award in 2023.

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Gori Gesha forest, Ethiopia |
| **Introduction to Thailand** | Approximately 2005 (Kaleb Jordan, seeds from Hacienda La Esmeralda, Panama) |
| **Genetic type** | Pure Arabica (Ethiopian landrace selection) |

### Key Characteristics

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Minimum altitude** | 1,200 masl; ideally 1,500+ masl |
| **SCA score range** | 87-95+ points |
| **Auction price** | Up to 10,000 THB/kg (Baan Maneepruek, 2023) |
| **Yield** | 40-80 kg/rai (250-500 kg/ha) — very low |
| **CLR resistance** | **SUSCEPTIBLE** — delicate plants require careful management |
| **Lifespan** | 20-30+ years |
| **Current share** | 2-5% — small but fastest-growing segment |

**Key Growing Sites:** Doi Chang (1,200-1,500 masl), Baan Maneepruek/Nan (1,400-1,600 masl), Doi Inthanon (1,400-1,700 masl)

**Typical Cup Profile (Thai Geisha):**
- Aroma: White flowers (jasmine, orange blossom), pink floral
- Flavor: Clean, refreshing acidity; citrus, berries, peach, bergamot
- Aftertaste: Long-lasting sweetness, tea-like; Earl Grey tea notes
- Nan Geisha specific: "Elegant pink floral aromatics, layered berry sweetness, Earl Grey tea, refreshing mint-like coolness" (Pacamara Coffee)

### Competition Results

- 2023 Thai Specialty Coffee Awards winner: Baan Maneepruek Geisha (Natural process)
- 2025 Thai Specialty Coffee Awards winner: Tuanthong Homdok, Nan, Geisha variety
- Thailand's inaugural Cup of Excellence 2025: Geisha varieties dominated top lots (87+ points)
- Catimor lots also scored competitively at 86.73 (COE 2025)

## SL28 / SL34 — The Kenyan Connection

### Overview

SL28 and SL34 are Kenyan selections that have a unique connection to Northern Thailand through the Chiang Mai 80 variety, which was backcrossed with SL28. While they are not widely planted as pure varieties in Thailand, their genetics are already embedded in the dominant Chiang Mai 80 cultivar, giving it improved cup quality over standard Catimor. Pure SL28/SL34 trials are very limited due to their susceptibility to CLR, which is endemic in Northern Thailand.

| Parameter | SL28 | SL34 |
|-----------|------|------|
| **Origin** | Kenya (Scott Laboratories, 1931) | Kenya (Scott Laboratories) |
| **SCA score range** | 85-90+ points | 83-88 points |
| **Yield** | Low | Medium |
| **CLR resistance** | Susceptible | Susceptible |
| **Current adoption** | less than 1% as pure variety; SL28 genetics present in approximately 70% of trees via Chiang Mai 80 | Very limited |

## Jember / S795 — The Historical Introduction

### Overview

S795 (known as "Jember" in Indonesia) was one of the first coffee leaf rust-resistant Arabica varieties, listed by Tummakate (1985) as an important Arabica variety introduced to Thailand from India. Its rust resistance has been largely overcome by evolving rust races, making it a risky choice for new plantings in CLR-endemic Northern Thailand. However, its Liberica-derived root system strength could be valuable for future breeding programs targeting drought resistance.

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Origin** | India (Coffee Research Station, Balehonnur, 1946-1948) |
| **Parentage** | S288 (Kent x Liberica/Arabica natural hybrid) x Typica/Kent |
| **Genetic type** | Introgressed (contains Liberica genes via S288) |
| **SCA score range** | 78-84 points |
| **Yield** | High (1,000-1,500 kg/ha in India) |
| **CLR resistance** | **DEGRADED** — originally resistant but new rust races have overcome resistance |
| **Current share** | less than 2% — very minor |

## Northern Thailand Context

The critical constraint for Arabica cultivation in Northern Thailand is that the highlands are lower than Arabica's native Ethiopian elevations (approximately 1,800 masl). The average highland elevation is only 1,000-1,200 masl, which is workable but at the lower end of ideal. Coffee leaf rust (Hemileia vastatrix) arrived in 1975, devastating early Caturra and Typica plantings, and this event fundamentally shaped the variety landscape — driving dominance toward rust-resistant Catimor/Chiang Mai 80. Shade-grown coffee systems integrating with the miang (fermented tea) tradition provide income resilience, with farmers harvesting coffee when prices are favorable and tea when they are not. The 2016 processing revolution proved that even Catimor can achieve specialty grade with controlled fermentation methods, opening new market opportunities for the region's predominantly Catimor-based production.

---

## Practical Recommendations

1. **For most farmers (800-1,200m):** Plant Chiang Mai 80 — it offers the best balance of rust resistance, yield, and achievable specialty quality, with the widest support network (DOA, Royal Project, nurseries).
2. **For specialty-focused farmers (1,200m+):** Consider Typica or Geisha if you have shade trees and can manage rust risk; the cup quality premium can be substantial (280-1,000+ THB/kg vs. 80-120 THB/kg commodity).
3. **For innovation-minded farmers:** Explore DOA Chiang Mai 1 (newer variety, higher cup score 76.75/100, higher yield 3.8-4 kg/tree) or Royal Project RPF-C3/C4 (compact, high-yielding, registered 2021).
4. **Avoid S795/Jember** for new plantings — rust resistance has degraded significantly.
5. **Invest in processing innovation:** The 2016 revolution proved Catimor can reach specialty grade through SCM, yeast, and other controlled fermentation methods.
