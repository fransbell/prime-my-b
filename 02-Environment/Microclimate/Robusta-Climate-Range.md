# Robusta Coffee Climate Range

> **Summary**: The optimal and tolerable climate parameters for Robusta coffee (Coffea canephora) cultivation, with comparison to Arabica and specific analysis of Robusta viability in Northern Thailand — covering temperature, rainfall, humidity, altitude, soil, Thai varieties, and economic considerations.

---

## Overview

Robusta coffee (Coffea canephora) is the world's second most cultivated coffee species, accounting for roughly 40% of global production. It evolved in the warm, humid lowland forests of West and Central Africa — a fundamentally different environment from Arabica's Ethiopian highland origins. This evolutionary history gives Robusta significantly greater heat tolerance and disease resistance, but also makes it more demanding of water and less tolerant of cold. Despite its "robust" name, recent research (Kath et al., 2020) has revealed that Robusta's optimal temperature for yield is 1.5–9°C lower than previously assumed, meaning many current Robusta-growing regions may already be above optimal temperatures.

In Thailand, Robusta is overwhelmingly a Southern crop — Chumphon, Surat Thani, Ranong, and Nakhon Si Thammarat produce approximately 95% of the nation's Robusta. However, pioneering farmers in Northern Thailand are exploring Fine Robusta cultivation at lower elevations (200–500m) as an alternative to corn, creating a new frontier for Thai coffee. Understanding Robusta's precise climate envelope is essential for evaluating these opportunities and designing monitoring systems for both traditional and emerging Robusta regions.

## Temperature Requirements

### Optimal Temperature Range

| Parameter | Value | Source |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| **Traditional optimal range** | 22–30°C | Matiello 1998; Willson 1999; widely cited |
| **Refined optimal range** | 22–26°C | MDPI Agronomy 2025; modern sources |
| **Narrow optimum (highest yield)** | 22–28°C | DaMatta & Ramalho 2006 |
| **Peak yield temperature (Kath 2020)** | <20.5°C mean annual | Kath et al., *Global Change Biology* |

**Critical note on Kath et al. (2020)**: This landmark study demonstrated that Robusta's optimal temperature for yield is significantly lower than the traditional 22–30°C range. The authors found yields decline substantially at mean annual temperatures above ~20.5°C. This finding has major implications: many current Robusta regions may already be above optimal, and climate change poses a more serious threat than previously modeled.

### Temperature Tolerance

| Threshold | Temperature | Effect |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Growth stops | <10°C | Plant ceases vegetative growth |
| Cold damage | <5–10°C (prolonged) | Leaf damage, dieback |
| Lethal cold | <0°C (frost) | Severe damage or death — Robusta has NO frost tolerance |
| Heat stress onset | >30°C (prolonged) | Reduced photosynthesis, yield decline |
| Severe heat damage | >35°C (sustained) | Leaf scorching, flower abortion, significant yield loss |

### Robusta vs. Arabica Temperature Comparison

| Parameter | Robusta | Arabica |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Optimal mean annual | 22–26°C | 18–22°C |
| Comfortable range | 20–30°C | 15–24°C |
| Growth stops below | 10°C | 5°C |
| Lethal cold | ~0°C | ~−2°C |
| Photosynthesis declines above | ~30°C | ~25°C |
| Heat tolerance advantage | +4–6°C over Arabica | Baseline |

## Rainfall Requirements

### Optimal Rainfall

| Parameter | Robusta | Arabica |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Optimal annual rainfall** | 2,000–2,500 mm | 1,200–1,800 mm |
| **Acceptable range** | 1,500–3,000 mm | 1,000–2,000 mm |
| **Dangerous minimum** | <1,200 mm | <800 mm |

Robusta requires significantly more water than Arabica — its optimal range of 2,000–2,500mm/year is roughly 50% higher than Arabica's. This reflects its lowland rainforest origins where water was abundant year-round.

### Drought Tolerance: A Common Misconception

Despite its "robust" name, Robusta has **worse drought tolerance than Arabica** in many respects. Research from Vietnam's Central Highlands shows Robusta requires 3–5 supplemental irrigations of 500–600 m³/ha each during the 5–6 month dry season. Studies comparing drought responses have found Arabica plants sometimes resist drought better than Robusta varieties. Robusta exhibits "drought-avoidance traits" rather than true drought tolerance — it avoids stress through deeper rooting but suffers more when water is truly limited. This is critical for Northern Thailand, where annual rainfall (1,100–1,700mm) falls well below Robusta's optimal range.

### Rainfall Timing

Robusta requires a distinct dry period of 2–3 months after harvest for flower bud differentiation, similar to Arabica. However, unlike Arabica (which is self-pollinated), Robusta is **cross-pollinated** and requires dry, calm weather during flowering for successful insect-mediated pollination. Year-round rain with no dry season leads to prolonged, unsynchronized harvest and low yields.

## Humidity Requirements

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Optimal range** | 70–90% RH | Higher than Arabica's 60–80% |
| **Minimum acceptable** | ~60% | Below this, stress increases |
| **Comfortable range** | 75–85% | Most commonly cited |

Robusta requires higher humidity than Arabica due to its lowland tropical origin. The key advantage is its natural resistance to Coffee Leaf Rust (CLR) through the Shrz gene, meaning it can tolerate higher humidity zones where Arabica would be devastated by disease. However, humidity above 85% can promote other fungal diseases including black rot and pink disease.

## Altitude Requirements

| Parameter | Global | Thailand |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Typical range** | 0–800m ASL | Southern: 50–400m |
| **Optimal range** | 200–600m | N. Thailand: 200–500m (emerging) |
| **Upper limit** | ~1,000–1,200m (equatorial) | Not applicable in N. Thailand |
| **Vietnam Central Highlands** | 400–800m | — |
| **India** | 500–1,000m | — |

Robusta is typically grown at lower altitudes than Arabica because it evolved in lowland tropical rainforests, has zero frost tolerance (lower altitudes in the tropics are frost-free), requires warmer temperatures matching its 22–26°C preference, and is outcompeted by Arabica at higher altitudes where Arabica produces better quality and commands higher prices.

## Soil Requirements

| Parameter | Robusta | Arabica |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Optimal pH** | 5.5–6.5 | 5.0–6.0 |
| **Tolerable pH range** | 4.5–8.0 | 4.5–6.5 |
| **Soil type** | Loamy or volcanic, well-draining | Sandy loam, free-draining |
| **Depth** | 1–2m+ for extensive root system | >1m |
| **Organic matter** | High preferred | 8–12% ideal |

Robusta tolerates a wider pH range than Arabica, particularly on the alkaline side. It requires deeper soils (1–2m+) to support its extensive root system. Heavy clay soils are unsuitable unless well-drained.

## Robusta Varieties in Thailand

### Chumphon Horticultural Research Center (CHRC)

The CHRC, established in 1960 under the Department of Agriculture, is Thailand's primary Robusta research institution. Located in Chumphon Province with ~280 hectares and ~200 staff, it collaborates with Nestlé on clone selection, breeding, and distribution of planting material. Thai Robusta is primarily based on open-pollinated seedling populations with significant genetic diversity. Formal cultivar names are primarily in Thai-language DOA publications; farmers typically receive vegetatively propagated clones from CHRC or DOAE extension services.

### Key Developments

- **Ranong Coffee**: Robusta from Ranong Province has received GI (Geographical Indication) status — retail price rose from ~80 THB/kg pre-GI to ~600 THB/kg, demonstrating the premium potential of origin-branded Robusta
- **Fine Robusta Movement**: Pioneering farmers like Kaleb Jordan (Nan Province) and Fuadi Pitsuwan (Beanspire Coffee) are producing specialty-grade Robusta using advanced processing methods
- **World Coffee Research (WCR)**: Launched a global Robusta breeding program in 2024; Thailand is part of the international network
- **Coffee++ Project**: Thai-German Cooperation focused on climate change adaptation for Robusta farmers in Chumphon and Ranong

### Robusta Growing Regions

| Region | Province(s) | Production Share | Notes |
|--------|------------|-----------------|-------|
| **Southern Thailand** | Chumphon, Surat Thani, Nakhon Si Thammarat, Ranong, Phang Nga, Krabi | ~95% | Heart of Thai Robusta; ~80,000 tonnes/year |
| **Northern Thailand** | Nan (emerging) | <5% | Fine Robusta pioneers; lower elevations |

## Robusta for Northern Thailand

### Viability Assessment

Robusta is viable in Northern Thailand only in specific lower-elevation areas. The mountainous terrain that makes the North ideal for Arabica means Robusta-suitable land is limited to valley floors and lower slopes.

| Area | Elevation | Viability | Notes |
|------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **Nan Province lowlands** | 200–500m | Proven | Kaleb Jordan's Fine Robusta project active |
| **Lampang valleys** | 250–500m | Good potential | Warm, lower elevation |
| **Phrae lowlands** | 150–400m | Good potential | Suitable thermal regime |
| **Uttaradit lowlands** | 50–300m | Good potential | Among lowest elevations in the North |
| **Tak (lower areas)** | 200–500m | Good potential | Border area with existing agriculture |
| **Chiang Mai lowlands** | 300–500m | Marginal | Cold air drainage risk in valleys |
| **Chiang Rai lowlands** | 300–500m | Marginal | Cool season may drop too low |

### Climate Match: Northern Lowlands vs. Robusta Optimal

| Parameter | Northern Lowlands | Robusta Optimal | Match? |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|--------|
| Mean annual temp | 25–28°C | 22–26°C | Slightly above optimal |
| Mean min (cool season) | 12–18°C | >17°C | Can drop below optimal |
| Annual rainfall | 1,200–1,800 mm | 2,000–2,500 mm | **Below optimal** |
| Dry season length | 5–6 months | 2–3 months | **Too long** |
| Humidity | 60–80% | 70–90% | Lower end |
| Frost risk | Rare in lowlands, possible in valleys | Zero tolerance | Risk exists |

**Major challenges**: Rainfall is significantly lower than Robusta's optimum (deficit of 500–1,200mm), and the dry season is 2–3 months longer than ideal. Supplemental irrigation is essential. The cool season's low night temperatures (12–15°C) approach Robusta's stress threshold.

### Economic Case

**Arguments for Robusta in Northern Thailand**: Lower elevation land is cheaper (often unsuitable for Arabica, currently used for corn); Robusta can replace corn as a perennial crop with lower long-term input costs; the Fine Robusta movement can command prices approaching commodity Arabica; disease resistance eliminates CLR risk; and Kaleb Jordan's Nan Province success demonstrates viability.

**Arguments against**: Insufficient rainfall requires irrigation investment; cool season stress may reduce yields; commodity Robusta sells for roughly half of Arabica; market infrastructure is oriented toward Arabica; and farmer experience with Robusta is limited in the North.

## Northern Thailand Context

Northern Thailand is fundamentally Arabica territory, but Robusta has a niche at lower elevations where Arabica cannot be grown profitably. The most promising areas are in Nan, Lampang, Phrae, and Uttaradit provinces at 200–500m elevation. Success depends on three critical factors: irrigation investment (to overcome the 500–1,200mm rainfall deficit), Fine Robusta market development (to overcome the commodity price gap), and careful site selection avoiding frost pockets where cold air drains into valleys during the cool season. The Fine Robusta movement, led by innovators like Kaleb Jordan, offers a pathway to specialty-grade prices that make Robusta economically viable even with irrigation costs.

---

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Consider Robusta only at 200–500m elevation** in Northern Thailand — above 500m, Arabica is almost always the better economic choice
2. **Plan for supplemental irrigation** — at minimum 3–5 applications of 500–600 m³/ha during the March–May dry season to support flowering and fruit set
3. **Avoid valley floor planting** — cold air drainage creates frost pockets lethal to Robusta; prefer gentle slopes or slightly elevated positions
4. **Target the Fine Robusta market** — commodity Robusta prices (80–120 THB/kg) barely cover production costs; specialty processing can command 300–600 THB/kg
5. **Use clonal planting material** from CHRC — vegetatively propagated clones offer consistent yield and quality versus open-pollinated seedlings
6. **Plant windbreaks** — Northern Thailand's cool season winds can cause stress; Leucaena or Gliricidia rows provide protection
7. **Monitor soil moisture closely** — Robusta's drought intolerance means even brief water stress during flowering can cause significant yield loss; IoT soil moisture sensors are essential
