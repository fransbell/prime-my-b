# Northern Thailand Weather Patterns

> **Summary**: Detailed monthly climate data for the four key coffee-producing provinces of Northern Thailand — Chiang Mai, Chiang Rai, Mae Hong Son, and Nan — including temperature, rainfall, sunshine, and humidity profiles relevant to coffee cultivation.

---

## Overview

Northern Thailand's climate is governed by the Asian monsoon system, creating three distinct seasons that directly shape coffee phenology. The cool-dry season (November–February) provides the temperature differential Arabica needs for quality cherry development, while the distinct dry period triggers synchronized flowering after the first rains. Understanding province-level climate variation is essential for matching coffee varieties to specific sites, predicting harvest timing, and designing sensor monitoring thresholds.

The four provinces covered here — Chiang Mai, Chiang Rai, Mae Hong Son, and Nan — represent the vast majority of Northern Thailand's Arabica coffee production. While they share the same monsoon rhythm, significant differences in total rainfall, temperature extremes, and elevation make each province uniquely suited to different coffee-growing strategies.

---

## Climate Classification

All four provinces fall under tropical climate classifications, but highland areas above ~1,000m shift into the humid subtropical (Cwa) zone that is most critical for Arabica quality.

| Province | Lowland Classification | Highland (1,000m+) |
|----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Chiang Mai | Aw (Tropical savanna) | Cwa (Humid subtropical) |
| Chiang Rai | Am (Tropical monsoon) | Cwa (Humid subtropical) |
| Mae Hong Son | Aw (Tropical savanna) | Cwa (Humid subtropical) |
| Nan | Aw (Tropical savanna) | Cwa (Humid subtropical) |

### Monsoon System

| Season | Period | Characteristics |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| **Cool/Dry Season** | November–February | NE monsoon; clear skies; low humidity; cool mornings (10–16°C); hot afternoons (27–33°C); minimal rainfall |
| **Hot/Dry Season** | March–May | Pre-monsoon; extreme heat (35–40°C max); increasing humidity; sporadic thunderstorms from April; burning season haze |
| **Rainy/Wet Season** | May–October | SW monsoon; heavy rainfall; high humidity (75–90%); reduced sunshine; peak rain August–September; tropical storms possible |

### Coffee-Growing Areas

- **Elevation range**: 800–1,500m (up to 1,900m at Doi Ang Khang)
- **Key areas**: Doi Inthanon (up to 2,565m), Doi Pui/Doi Suthep (1,000–1,685m), Doi Ang Khang (1,400–1,900m), Mae Rim, Samoeng, Mae Taeng
- **Primary variety**: Arabica (Typica, Caturra, Catuai, Bourbon, Geisha)
- **Royal Project Foundation** operates research stations at Doi Ang Khang and Doi Pui

### Frost Risk

Frost occurs annually at high elevations (1,400m+): Doi Inthanon, Doi Ang Khang, and Doi Chiang Dao. Timing is November–February, with peaks in December–January. Hoar frost (not ground freeze) is most common; summit temperatures can drop to 0–4°C. Young Arabica plants at 1,000m+ can suffer cold damage; frost during flowering is particularly damaging.

### Microclimate Features

- **Valley inversion effect**: Cold air settles in the Ping River valley at night, making the city surprisingly cool on December–February mornings
- **Rain shadow**: Mountains west of Chiang Mai create partial rain shadow, making this province drier than Chiang Rai
- **Doi Inthanon microclimate**: Summit area has montane cloud forest; temperatures 10–15°C cooler than valley; frequent mist/fog
- **Burning season (Feb–Apr)**: Agricultural burning causes severe PM2.5 pollution, affecting plant health and photosynthesis

### Coffee-Growing Areas

- **Elevation range**: 800–1,500m (optimal 1,000–1,400m for Arabica)
- **Key areas**: Doi Chang (1,200–1,500m), Doi Tung (800–1,400m), Doi Wawee (1,000–1,200m), Mae Suai, Wiang Pa Pao, Pangkhon Village
- **Chiang Rai is Thailand's most important Arabica-producing province**
- **Doi Chang** is internationally recognized for specialty-grade Arabica
- **Doi Tung** coffee is part of the Royal Project's flagship brand

### Frost Risk

Rare at coffee-growing elevations (800–1,400m); occasional frost at peaks above 1,500m. Doi Pha Tang and Doi Mae Salong (~1,500–1,800m) can experience near-freezing temperatures December–January. Much lower frost risk than Chiang Mai's highest peaks.

### Microclimate Features

- **Much wetter than Chiang Mai** — receives nearly 2× the annual rainfall (Am vs Aw classification)
- **Mekong River influence**: Proximity to the Mekong and Myanmar/Laos borders creates different humidity patterns
- **Doi Chang microclimate**: Unique terroir with well-drained volcanic/loam soils, consistent afternoon cloud cover during wet season
- **Misty mountain conditions** at Doi Tung and Doi Wawee slow cherry ripening, enhancing flavor complexity
- **Less smoke/pollution** than Chiang Mai during burning season due to different wind patterns

### Coffee-Growing Areas

- **Elevation range**: 800–1,500m
- **Key areas**: Pang Mapha, Pai highlands, Khun Yuam, Mae La Noi, Baan Huay Hom
- **Primarily Arabica**: Typica, Caturra, Catuai, Bourbon, and Geisha cultivars
- **Smaller-scale production** compared to Chiang Rai; many family-owned farms
- **Shade-grown and organic practices** are common

### Frost Risk

Possible at highest elevations above 1,500m (e.g., peaks near Pang Mapha). Very rare at coffee-growing elevations (800–1,200m). Lowest frost risk of the four provinces due to lower latitude and mountain orientation.

### Microclimate Features

- **Shan State influence**: Located in a valley surrounded by Myanmar's Shan Hills; unique air flow patterns
- **Pai highlands**: Narrow valleys with strong temperature inversions; mornings can be very cool even at moderate elevation
- **Highest monsoon humidity** of the four provinces (90%+ in August)
- **Afternoon cloud cover** at higher elevations provides natural shade for coffee
- **Significantly drier cool season** than Chiang Rai (lower total annual rainfall)
- **Smoke/haze** from agricultural burning is a significant issue February–April

### Coffee-Growing Areas

- **Elevation range**: 800–1,600m
- **Key areas**: Doi Phu Kha National Park (1,400–1,600m), Doi Suan Ya Luang (~1,600m), Pua District, Bo Kluea, Santisuk
- **Emerging specialty coffee region** — Nan Geisha coffee is gaining international recognition at 1,400–1,600m elevation
- **Certified Q Graders** among Nan coffee farmers
- **Organic farming** is common; smallholder production

### Frost Risk

Very rare at typical coffee elevations; Doi Phu Kha summit (~1,980m) may experience near-freezing December–January. Lower overall frost risk than Chiang Mai highlands. Cool nights at 1,400m+ can slow cherry maturation, potentially enhancing quality.

### Microclimate Features

- **Most easterly of the four provinces** — receives slightly different monsoon timing
- **Doi Phu Kha influence**: Limestone karst mountains create localized rain patterns; eastern slopes receive earlier monsoon rain
- **Nan River valley**: Creates temperature inversions, especially in the narrow northern valleys
- **Relatively remote** — less air pollution than Chiang Mai/Chiang Rai during burning season
- **Good diurnal range** at higher elevations: warm days (25–30°C) and cool nights (12–16°C) during dry season — ideal for Arabica quality

## Coffee Phenology and Seasonal Patterns

### Arabica Coffee Annual Cycle in Northern Thailand

| Stage | Period | Weather Conditions |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| **Flowering initiation** | February–April | Hot, dry → first rains trigger blossom; temperature 25–35°C; humidity rising |
| **Main flowering** | March–May | First monsoon showers stimulate flowering; 1–3 flowerings typical |
| **Fruit development** | June–September | Heavy rain and high humidity; cherries swell; risk of disease (CBD, leaf rust) |
| **Cherry maturation** | October–November | Rain diminishes; cooler temperatures; sugar accumulation in cherries |
| **Harvest** | November–February | Cool, dry season; hand-picking over multiple passes; ideal drying weather December–January |
| **Post-harvest pruning** | February–March | Dry period; trees recover before next flowering |

## Highland Adjustment for Coffee Zone Data

All temperature and rainfall data above represents lowland meteorological station readings. Highland coffee farms require the following adjustments:

| Parameter | Adjustment | Reason |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| Maximum temperature | Subtract 3–5°C | Lapse rate (~6.5°C/1,000m) |
| Minimum temperature | Subtract 2–4°C | Lapse rate (slightly less than max) |
| Rainfall | Add 10–20% | Orographic enhancement |
| Sunshine hours | Reduce 15–25% | Cloud/fog at altitude |
| Humidity | Add 5–10% | Cloud/fog/mist |

## Practical Recommendations

1. **Site selection**: Prioritize 1,000–1,400m elevation for Arabica; below 800m is too warm for quality production
2. **Disease management**: Chiang Rai's high rainfall (1,700–2,172mm) demands aggressive CLR monitoring during wet season; Chiang Mai's drier conditions reduce but do not eliminate risk
3. **Harvest planning**: Chiang Mai and Mae Hong Son offer the best drying conditions (low rainfall Nov–Feb); Chiang Rai's wetter harvest may require covered drying infrastructure
4. **Frost avoidance**: In Chiang Mai, avoid planting above 1,600m unless frost protection is available; other provinces have lower risk
5. **Burning season mitigation**: February–April PM2.5 can reduce photosynthesis by 10–30%; consider location upwind of major burning areas or plan for irrigation during this stress period
6. **Irrigation investment**: All provinces experience a 5–6 month dry season; supplemental irrigation during March–May is essential for consistent flowering and fruit set
